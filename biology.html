Top Exam Tips

1. Section A surplus rule! If you give more answers than asked AND one is wrong, it cancels a correct answer. Only give the exact number requested.
2. Learn mandatory experiments. Section B is based on experiments you did in class. Very predictable - know procedures, results, controls.
3. Use scientific terminology. "Osmosis" not "water movement". "Mitochondrion" not "powerhouse". Specific terms earn marks.
4. Label ALL diagrams. Diagrams without labels earn reduced marks. Label at least 3 parts when asked.
5. Answer the question asked. "Name" = one word. "Describe" = detailed explanation. "Outline" = brief explanation.
6. Section C has options. Q16 and Q17 each have 4 parts (a,b,c,d) - answer any 2 from each. Pick your strongest topics.
7. Start each Section C question on a new page. Write the question number in the box at top of page.

Q1: Nutrition - Section A 20 marks

Answer any 5 from parts (a) to (f). Each part worth 4 marks.

(a) General formula for carbohydrates 4

Answer: Cx(H2O)y
Why? Carbohydrates contain carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. The ratio of H:O is always 2:1 (like water). Examples: glucose C₆H₁₂O₆ fits the pattern C₆(H₂O)₆.

(b) Four chemical elements in all proteins 4

Answer: Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N)
Remember CHON. All proteins contain these four elements. Some also contain sulfur (S) in amino acids like cysteine.

(c) One structural role of proteins 4

Answer: Muscles / tendons / ligaments / hair / skin / nails (any one)
Structural vs functional. Structural proteins provide support and shape (collagen, keratin). Functional proteins include enzymes, antibodies, hormones.

(d) Small subunits that make protein 4

Answer: Amino acids
20 different amino acids can combine in different sequences to make thousands of different proteins. They link via peptide bonds.

(e) One water-soluble vitamin 4

Answer: Vitamin B (or B1, B2, B12 etc.) / Vitamin C
Water-soluble vs fat-soluble. Water-soluble: B and C (not stored, need daily intake). Fat-soluble: A, D, E, K (stored in body fat).

(f) One trace element found in food 4

Answer: Copper (Cu) / Iron (Fe) / Zinc (Zn) / Iodine (I)
Trace elements are needed in tiny amounts. Iron is in haemoglobin. Iodine is needed for thyroid hormones. Zinc aids enzyme function.

Q2: Plant Root Structure - Section A 20 marks

Diagram shows a longitudinal section through a plant root with structures X, Y, Z labelled.

(a) Structure X and its function 6

Name: Root hair [3]
Function: Absorption of water or minerals [3]
Root hairs are extensions of epidermal cells. They increase surface area for absorption from soil. Short-lived (few weeks).

(b) Tissue type Y and its function 6

Name: Vascular tissue [3]
Function: Transport of water / minerals / food [3]
Vascular tissue includes xylem (water & minerals up) and phloem (sugars up/down). Forms the central core in roots.

(c) Tissue Z where rapid mitosis occurs 3

Answer: Meristematic (tissue)
Meristem = region of rapid cell division. Found at root tips and shoot tips. Cells are small, thin-walled, actively dividing.

(d) Draw transverse section at dashed line 5

Drawing: Circle with vascular tissue in centre [3]
Labels: Any two from: dermal tissue, vascular tissue, ground tissue, root hair [2×1]
Cross-section shows: Outer epidermis (dermal), cortex (ground tissue), central vascular cylinder. Different from stem where vascular bundles are arranged in a ring.

Q3: Aerobic & Anaerobic Respiration - Section A 20 marks

Diagram shows a mitochondrion where stage 2 of aerobic respiration occurs.

(a) Name the cell organelle shown 2

Answer: Mitochondrion
Mitochondria (plural) are the "powerhouses" of the cell. They have a double membrane - cristae (inner folds) increase surface area for ATP production.

(b) Name the cycle in stage 2 3

Answer: Krebs cycle
Stage 1 = glycolysis (cytosol). Stage 2 = Krebs cycle (mitochondria). Krebs cycle produces CO₂, NADH, FADH₂, and some ATP.

(c) What does ATP stand for? 3

Answer: Adenosine triphosphate
ATP is the energy currency of cells. When the third phosphate bond breaks (ATP → ADP + P), energy is released for cellular work.

(d) Function of NAD⁺ 3

Answer: Carries (high energy) electrons and protons
NAD⁺ is an electron carrier. It accepts electrons (becomes NADH), then delivers them to the electron transport chain where ATP is made.

(e) Condition for anaerobic respiration 3

Answer: Lack of oxygen / described
When oxygen runs out (intense exercise, waterlogged soil), cells switch to anaerobic respiration. Much less efficient - only 2 ATP per glucose vs 38 ATP aerobically.

(f) Where anaerobic respiration occurs 3

Answer: Cytosol
Anaerobic = no mitochondria needed. Occurs in cytosol (cell fluid). Just glycolysis, no Krebs cycle or electron transport.

(g) One main product of anaerobic respiration 3

Answer: Lactic acid (animals) / Ethanol (yeast, plants)
In muscle cells: glucose → lactic acid (causes muscle fatigue). In yeast: glucose → ethanol + CO₂ (brewing, baking).

Q4: Cell Membrane & Structure - Section A 20 marks

Diagram shows cell membrane ultrastructure with parts X and Y labelled.

(a) Name parts X and Y 4

X: Phospholipid [2]
Y: Protein [2]
Fluid mosaic model: Phospholipid bilayer (double layer) with proteins embedded. Phospholipids have hydrophilic heads (out) and hydrophobic tails (in).

(b) Function of cell membrane 3

Answer: Controls what substances pass through / selective permeability
Semi-permeable membrane allows some substances through (water, small molecules) but blocks others (large molecules, ions without channels).

(c)(i) Term for cells without membrane-bound organelles 3

Answer: Prokaryotic
Prokaryotes = bacteria. No nucleus, no mitochondria, no chloroplasts. DNA free in cytoplasm. Small and simple.

(c)(ii) Term for cells with membrane-bound organelles 3

Answer: Eukaryotic
Eukaryotes = animals, plants, fungi, protists. Have nucleus, mitochondria, and other organelles. Larger and more complex than prokaryotes.

(d)(i) Sketch plant cell - label membrane & wall 5

Sketch: Showing cell membrane inside cell wall [3]
Labels: Cell membrane [1], Cell wall [1]
Plant cells have BOTH. Cell wall (outer, rigid, cellulose) + cell membrane (inner, controls entry). Animal cells only have membrane.

(d)(ii) One function of plant cell wall 2

Answer: Gives shape (to the cell) / Protection
Cell wall prevents cell bursting when water enters by osmosis. Made of cellulose. Fully permeable (not selective like membrane).

Q5: Bioprocessing & Growth Curves - Section A 20 marks

Image shows equipment for food processing with bacteria. Graph shows population growth curve with stages X and Y.

(a) Common name for equipment 3

Answer: Bioreactor
Bioreactor (fermenter) provides controlled conditions for microorganism growth. Used in brewing, antibiotic production, yogurt making.

(b) Two factors controlled (not nutrients) 6

Answer: Any two from: pH / temperature / pressure / oxygen / stirring rate [2×3]
Optimum conditions maximize growth and product yield. Too hot = enzymes denature. Wrong pH = enzymes don't work efficiently.

(c) Name stages X and Y 6

Stage X: Log (phase) [3]
Stage Y: Stationary (phase) [3]
Growth phases: Lag (adapting) → Log (rapid exponential growth) → Stationary (birth rate = death rate) → Death (toxins accumulate, nutrients depleted).

(d) Type of food processing technique 3

Answer: Continuous (culture)
Continuous vs batch. Continuous = fresh nutrients added constantly, population stays steady. Batch = sealed vessel, population rises then falls.

(e) Draw how line continues if nutrients run out 2

Answer: Line drops (death phase)
No nutrients = death phase. Bacteria die faster than they reproduce. Population crashes. Toxins accumulate.

Q6: Human Digestive System - Section A 20 marks

Diagram shows digestive system with parts A and B labelled.

(a) Name parts A and B 4

A: Oesophagus [2]
B: Stomach [2]
Oesophagus = food pipe connecting mouth to stomach. Stomach = muscular bag where protein digestion begins.

(b) Method food travels through A 6

Name: Peristalsis [3]
Description: (Involuntary) muscular contractions (that push food) [3]
Peristalsis = wave-like muscle contractions. Circular muscles contract behind food bolus, longitudinal muscles contract ahead. Works even upside down.

(c) pH change from A to B 2

Answer: Lowers (becomes more acidic)
Oesophagus ≈ neutral pH 7. Stomach ≈ pH 2 (very acidic). HCl acid kills bacteria and activates pepsin enzyme.

(d) Pancreatic enzyme that digests lipids 3

Answer: Lipase
Lipase breaks down lipids (fats) into fatty acids and glycerol. Produced in pancreas, works in small intestine (alkaline conditions).

(e) Draw and label internal structure of villus 5

Drawing: Villus / lacteal / blood vessels - any two [3]
Labels: Villus / lacteal / capillary - any two [2×1]
Villus = finger-like projection in small intestine. Contains blood capillaries (absorb glucose, amino acids) and lacteal (absorbs fats). Huge surface area for absorption.

Q7: Scientific Method - Section A 20 marks

(a) Testable statement is called 2

Answer: Hypothesis
Hypothesis = educated guess that can be tested by experiment. Must be falsifiable (can be proven wrong).

(b) How to test the statement 3

Answer: Conducting an experiment
Experiment tests hypothesis by changing one variable (independent) and measuring effect on another (dependent). All other variables kept constant.

(c) Function of scientific control 3

Answer: A comparison to the experiment / baseline
Control = identical setup without the variable being tested. Lets you compare and see if your variable caused the effect.

(d) Two limitations of scientific method 6

Answer: Any two from: Accidental discovery / application to nature / (human) bias / human error / constrained by existing knowledge / constrained by technology / ability to interpret results / experimental design [2×3]
Not all discoveries follow method. Penicillin was accidental. Some things can't be tested ethically. Observer bias affects results.

(e) Where biologists publish results 3

Answer: Scientific journal
Peer-reviewed journals ensure quality. Other scientists check the work before publication. Examples: Nature, Science, Cell.

(f) What is a theory? 3

Answer: Supported (or tested) hypothesis
Theory ≠ guess. A theory is a hypothesis tested many times with consistent results. Example: Cell theory, Evolution theory.

Q8: Ecosystem Study (Mandatory Experiment) - Section B 30 marks

(a)(i) What is an ecosystem? 2

Answer: Organisms and their environment
Ecosystem = all living organisms (biotic) and non-living factors (abiotic) interacting in an area. Examples: woodland, pond, rocky shore.

(a)(ii) What aids identification of organisms? 4

Answer: Key (identification key / dichotomous key)
Keys use paired statements to identify species. Each choice narrows down possibilities until species is identified.

(b)(i) Quantitative study of named plant species 12

Named plant species [3]
Equipment: Used quadrat or line transect or belt transect and correct use [3]
Method: Counted and repeated [3]
Calculation: By percentage cover or percentage frequency [3]
Quadrat method: Throw 50cm × 50cm frame randomly. Count how many squares contain the plant. Repeat 10+ times. Calculate % frequency = (squares with plant ÷ total squares) × 100.

(b)(ii) Two abiotic factors and how measured 12

Factor 1: Correctly named (e.g., light intensity) [3]
Method 1: Matching method (e.g., light meter) [3]
Factor 2: Correctly named (e.g., soil pH) [3]
Method 2: Matching method (e.g., pH meter or indicator) [3]
Common abiotic factors: Light (light meter), Temperature (thermometer), Soil pH (pH meter/indicator), Soil moisture (moisture meter), Wind speed (anemometer).

Q11: Plant Reproduction - Section C 60 marks

(a)(i) What is geotropism? 3

Answer: Growth response (of a plant) to gravity
Tropism = directional growth response to stimulus. Geo = earth/gravity. Roots show positive geotropism (grow down). Shoots show negative geotropism (grow up).

(a)(ii) Part that responds positively to geotropism 3

Answer: Root
Positive = grows toward stimulus. Roots grow toward gravity (downward). Ensures roots anchor plant and reach water.

(a)(iii) How does this benefit plants? 3

Answer: Obtain (more) water or minerals or anchorage
Survival advantage. Downward-growing roots find water underground and anchor plant securely.

(b)(i) Collective names for reproductive parts 6

1. Male parts: Stamen [3]
2. Female parts: Carpel [3]
Stamen = anther (makes pollen) + filament. Carpel = stigma + style + ovary.

(b)(ii) Part that holds embryo sac 3

Answer: Ovule (accept ovary)
Ovule is inside the ovary. After fertilisation, ovule becomes seed and ovary becomes fruit.

(b)(iii) Embryo sac develops from this cell 3

Answer: Megaspore mother (cell)
Megaspore mother cell is diploid. Undergoes meiosis to produce 4 haploid cells. 3 degenerate, 1 survives and divides by mitosis to form 8-nuclei embryo sac.

(b)(iv) Parts P and Q involved in double fertilisation 6

P: Polar nucleus [3]
Q: Egg (cell) [3]
Double fertilisation is unique to flowering plants. One sperm fuses with egg (→ zygote). Second sperm fuses with 2 polar nuclei (→ endosperm).

(b)(v) Why P and Q genetically identical 3

Answer: They were produced by mitosis
Mitosis produces genetically identical cells. All 8 nuclei in embryo sac came from mitosis of one megaspore.

(b)(vi) What P and Q develop into after fertilisation 6

P: Endosperm [3]
Q: Diploid zygote [3]
Endosperm = food store for embryo (triploid 3n). Zygote divides to form embryo (diploid 2n).

(c)(i) Two methods of seed dispersal 6

Answer: Any two from: Wind / animal / water / self (explosive) [2×3]
Examples: Wind (dandelion, sycamore), Animal (berries, burrs), Water (coconut), Explosive (gorse pod).

(c)(ii) Two advantages of seed dispersal 6

Answer: Any two from: Reduce competition / colonise new habitats / better chance of survival [2×3]
Why disperse? Seedlings under parent plant compete for light, water, nutrients. Spreading increases species range.

(c)(iii) Two advantages of dormancy 6

Answer: Any two from: Allows time for development of embryo / allows time for dispersal / allows survival in unfavourable conditions [2×3]
Dormancy = period when seed doesn't germinate even with water/warmth. Lets it survive winter or drought.

(c)(iv) Examples of asexual reproduction 6

1. Using leaves: Correct example (e.g., Bryophyllum) [3]
2. Using buds: Correct example (e.g., potato, daffodil) [3]
Vegetative propagation = asexual reproduction using plant parts. Leaves (Kalanchoe), Stems (runners in strawberry), Roots (dahlia), Buds (potato tuber).

Q12: Genetics & DNA - Section C 60 marks

(a)(i) Explain mutation 3

Answer: Change in the structure (or sequence) of DNA (or of a chromosome)
Mutation = permanent change in genetic material. Can be beneficial, harmful, or neutral. Source of genetic variation.

(a)(ii) Two causes of mutations 6

Answer: Any two from: Radiation / chemicals / viruses [2×3]
Mutagens increase mutation rate. UV radiation, X-rays, tobacco smoke, certain viruses (HPV). Most mutations during DNA replication are random errors.

(b)(i) Distinguish gene and allele 6

Gene: Region of chromosome (or DNA) that contains (genetic) code for (production of) a protein [3]
Allele: Form of a gene [3]
Example: Gene for eye colour has different alleles (brown, blue, green). You inherit one allele from each parent.

(b)(ii)1. Genotype ebony/normal heterozygous 3

Answer: EeNn
Heterozygous = two different alleles. E (ebony dominant) and e (black recessive), N (normal dominant) and n (curly recessive).

(b)(ii)2. Genotype black body/curly wings 3

Answer: eenn
Recessive phenotype = homozygous recessive genotype. Black is recessive (ee), curly is recessive (nn).

(b)(ii)3. Offspring genotypes and phenotypes 12

Cross: EeNn × eenn
EeNn: ebony, normal [3]
Eenn: ebony, curly [3]
eeNn: black, normal [3]
eenn: black, curly [3]
Use Punnett square. EeNn produces gametes: EN, En, eN, en. eenn produces only en. 4 different offspring types in 1:1:1:1 ratio.

(b)(iii) Term for genes on same chromosome 3

Answer: Linked
Linked genes are inherited together (don't follow Mendel's law of independent assortment). Closer together = less likely to separate by crossing over.

(c)(i) What does DNA stand for? 3

Answer: Deoxyribonucleic acid
DNA carries genetic information. Double helix structure discovered by Watson & Crick (1953).

(c)(ii) Purines and pyrimidines 6

Purines: Adenine (A) and Guanine (G) [3]
Pyrimidines: Cytosine (C) and Thymine (T) [3]
Base pairing rules: A pairs with T (2 H-bonds), G pairs with C (3 H-bonds). Purines are larger (double ring), pyrimidines smaller (single ring).

(c)(iii) One difference between DNA and RNA 3

Answer: DNA is double stranded, RNA is single stranded
OR DNA has deoxyribose sugar, RNA has ribose
OR DNA has thymine, RNA has uracil
RNA (ribonucleic acid) is temporary copy of DNA used to make proteins. mRNA, tRNA, rRNA are different types.

(c)(iv) Four steps in DNA profiling 12

1. Cells are broken down to release DNA [3]
2. DNA is cut into fragments using enzymes [3]
3. Fragments are separated based on size [3]
4. Pattern of fragments is analysed [3]
DNA profiling uses gel electrophoresis to separate fragments. Smaller fragments travel further. Pattern is unique to each individual (except identical twins). Used in forensics, paternity testing.
