Top Exam Tips

1. Section A surplus rule! If you give more answers than asked AND one is wrong, it cancels a correct answer. Only give the exact number requested.
2. Learn mandatory experiments. Section B is based on experiments you did in class. Very predictable - know procedures, results, controls.
3. Use scientific terminology. "Osmosis" not "water movement". "Mitochondrion" not "powerhouse". Specific terms earn marks.
4. Label ALL diagrams. Diagrams without labels earn reduced marks. Label at least 3 parts when asked.
5. Answer the question asked. "Name" = one word. "Describe" = detailed explanation. "Outline" = brief explanation.
6. Section C has options. Q16 and Q17 each have 4 parts (a,b,c,d) - answer any 2 from each. Pick your strongest topics.
7. Start each Section C question on a new page. Write the question number in the box at top of page.

Mark Scheme Decoder

Understanding how Biology examiners award marks helps you maximise your score.

General Marking Principles

PrincipleDetail
Key wordsExaminers look for specific scientific terms. Synonyms are usually accepted, but exact terms are safer.
Separate pointsEach mark is typically awarded for a separate point. Write one point per line or clearly separate them.
No penalty for extrasIf you give more points than asked for, the examiner marks the best ones. But contradictions can lose marks.
DiagramsDiagrams must be labelled to earn marks. A diagram alone without labels scores poorly.
DefinitionsMust be precise. "Osmosis = water moving" is too vague. Include direction (high to low water concentration) and membrane.
ExperimentsMust include method, controls, and expected results. Just naming the experiment isn't enough.

Section A - Short Questions (20 marks each)

Format: 5 parts, answer all. Usually 4 marks per part.
Typical structure: Define, name, state, give an example, draw and label.
Common errors: Vague definitions, missing labels on diagrams, giving functions when asked for locations.
Tip: These should take ~6 minutes each. Don't overthink — short, precise answers. One correct scientific term per mark.

Section B - Short Answer (30 marks each)

Format: Usually involves a practical/experiment scenario.
Key elements for full marks: State the method clearly, identify variables (independent, dependent, controlled), give expected results, draw conclusions.
Diagrams: Must be large, clear, and labelled. Each correct label = marks.
Tip: Always mention a control in experiment questions. "A control was set up using [same conditions minus the variable being tested]."

Section C - Long Questions (60 marks each)

Format: 3 parts (a), (b), (c), usually 18-24 marks each.
Parts often follow a pattern: (a) Definitions/basic knowledge, (b) Process description/experiment, (c) Application/higher-order thinking.
Marking: Each sub-part has allocated marks. Examiners use the "first correct answers" principle — if you give 6 answers and only 4 are needed, the first 4 encountered are marked.
Tip: Answer in bullet points or numbered lists. Makes it easy for the examiner to award marks. Write more points than required — extras won't lose marks (unless contradictory).

Golden Rules for Maximum Marks

1. Use scientific terminology. "Selectively permeable membrane" not "skin of the cell".
2. Label everything. Diagrams without labels lose most of their marks.
3. Separate your points. One idea per line. Don't bury answers in paragraphs.
4. State the obvious. If asked "What is the role of chlorophyll?", say "Chlorophyll absorbs light energy for photosynthesis." Don't assume the examiner knows what you mean.
5. Answer what's asked. "Name" = one word. "Explain" = definition + detail. "Describe" = step by step. "Discuss" = for and against.
6. Include controls in experiments. Always mention what you kept the same and what you changed.
7. Give units. Temperature in °C, concentration in M or %, time in minutes. Missing units can cost marks.
8. Don't leave blanks. Write something for every part. Even a partially correct answer earns marks.

Biology Glossary

Search for any term. All definitions written in plain language with exam-ready precision.

Active Transport
Movement of molecules across a membrane from low to high concentration, requiring energy (ATP). Against the concentration gradient.
Aerobic Respiration
Breakdown of glucose using oxygen to release energy (ATP). Occurs in the mitochondria. C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 38 ATP.
Allele
An alternative form of a gene. Each gene has two alleles (one from each parent). Can be dominant or recessive.
Anaerobic Respiration
Breakdown of glucose without oxygen. In animals: glucose → lactic acid + 2 ATP. In yeast: glucose → ethanol + CO₂ + 2 ATP.
Antibiotic
A chemical substance that kills or inhibits the growth of bacteria. Does NOT work on viruses. E.g., penicillin.
Antibody
A protein produced by white blood cells (lymphocytes) in response to an antigen. Specific to each antigen.
Antigen
A foreign substance (usually a protein on the surface of a pathogen) that triggers an immune response.
ATP
Adenosine triphosphate. The energy currency of the cell. Produced during respiration and used for cell activities.
Autotroph
An organism that makes its own food from inorganic substances (e.g., plants using photosynthesis).
Biodiversity
The variety of different species of organisms in an ecosystem. Higher biodiversity = more stable ecosystem.
Biome
A large ecological area with characteristic climate, plants, and animals. E.g., tropical rainforest, tundra, desert.
Bioreactor
A vessel in which microorganisms are grown under controlled conditions to produce useful products (e.g., enzymes, antibiotics, ethanol).
Carnivore
An organism that feeds on other animals. A secondary or tertiary consumer in a food chain.
Catalyst
A substance that speeds up a chemical reaction without being used up. Enzymes are biological catalysts.
Cell Membrane
The selectively permeable barrier surrounding all cells. Controls what enters and leaves the cell. Made of a phospholipid bilayer.
Cell Wall
A rigid outer layer found in plant cells (made of cellulose), fungi (chitin), and bacteria (peptidoglycan). Provides structural support.
Chlorophyll
The green pigment in chloroplasts that absorbs light energy for photosynthesis. Absorbs red and blue light, reflects green.
Chloroplast
The organelle where photosynthesis occurs in plant cells. Contains chlorophyll on thylakoid membranes.
Chromosome
A structure made of DNA and protein found in the nucleus. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). Carries genes.
Cohesion-Tension Theory
Explains water transport in plants. Water evaporates from leaves (transpiration), creating tension that pulls water up through xylem vessels in a continuous column.
Community
All the populations of different species living and interacting in a particular area.
Competition
Interaction where organisms compete for the same limited resources (food, space, mates, light). Can be intraspecific or interspecific.
Crossing Over
Exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes during meiosis I. Increases genetic variation.
Decomposer
An organism that breaks down dead organic matter, recycling nutrients back into the ecosystem. E.g., bacteria, fungi.
Denaturation
Loss of the 3D shape of a protein (especially an enzyme) due to heat or extreme pH. The active site changes shape.
Denitrification
Conversion of nitrates (NO₃⁻) back to atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) by denitrifying bacteria in waterlogged/anaerobic soil.
Diffusion
Net movement of molecules from a region of high concentration to low concentration. Passive — no energy required.
Diploid
A cell with two complete sets of chromosomes (2n). In humans, 2n = 46. Body cells are diploid.
DNA
Deoxyribonucleic acid. Double helix molecule carrying genetic instructions. Made of nucleotides (sugar, phosphate, base).
Dominant
An allele that is expressed in the phenotype when present in one or two copies. Represented by a capital letter.
Ecology
The study of the relationships between living organisms and their environment.
Ecosystem
A community of living organisms interacting with each other and their physical (abiotic) environment.
Endocrine System
The system of glands that produce hormones. Hormones travel in the blood and regulate body functions.
Enzyme
A biological catalyst (protein) that speeds up reactions by lowering activation energy. Specific to its substrate (lock and key model).
Eukaryotic
A cell with a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Includes animal, plant, and fungal cells.
Evolution
Change in the inherited characteristics of a population over successive generations through natural selection.
Fermentation
Anaerobic respiration in yeast. Glucose → Ethanol + CO₂ + 2 ATP. Used in brewing and baking.
Food Chain
A linear sequence showing energy transfer from one organism to the next. E.g., Grass → Rabbit → Fox.
Food Web
A network of interconnected food chains showing the complex feeding relationships in an ecosystem.
Gamete
A sex cell (sperm or egg). Haploid — contains half the chromosome number. Produced by meiosis.
Gene
A section of DNA that codes for a specific protein. Located on chromosomes.
Genetic Engineering
The deliberate modification of an organism's DNA by inserting genes from another organism. Used to produce insulin, GMOs.
Genotype
The genetic makeup of an organism (the alleles it carries). E.g., Bb (heterozygous).
Glycolysis
First stage of respiration. Occurs in the cytoplasm. Glucose (6C) is broken into 2 molecules of pyruvate (3C). Produces 2 ATP.
Habitat
The place where an organism lives. Provides food, shelter, and conditions for survival.
Haploid
A cell with one set of chromosomes (n). In humans, n = 23. Gametes (sex cells) are haploid.
Herbivore
An organism that feeds on plants. A primary consumer in a food chain.
Heterotroph
An organism that cannot make its own food. Must consume other organisms for energy. Includes all animals.
Heterozygous
Having two different alleles for a gene. E.g., Bb. The dominant allele is expressed.
Homeostasis
The maintenance of a constant internal environment in the body. E.g., temperature, blood sugar, water balance.
Homozygous
Having two identical alleles for a gene. E.g., BB (homozygous dominant) or bb (homozygous recessive).
Hormone
A chemical messenger produced by endocrine glands, carried in the blood, that affects target organs.
Hypothesis
A testable prediction or proposed explanation for an observation. Must be capable of being proven true or false.
Immobilised Enzyme
An enzyme that is attached to an inert material (e.g., alginate beads). Can be reused, works at higher temperatures.
Krebs Cycle
Second stage of aerobic respiration. Occurs in the matrix of mitochondria. Pyruvate is broken down, releasing CO₂ and producing ATP, NADH, FADH₂.
Meiosis
Cell division producing 4 genetically different haploid cells. Two divisions. Used to produce gametes. Introduces variation.
Metabolism
All the chemical reactions occurring in a living organism. Includes anabolism (building up) and catabolism (breaking down).
Mitochondria
The organelle where aerobic respiration occurs. The "powerhouse of the cell." Has a double membrane with inner folds (cristae).
Mitosis
Cell division producing 2 genetically identical diploid cells. Used for growth and repair. Stages: PMAT (Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase).
Mutation
A change in the base sequence of DNA. Can be spontaneous or caused by mutagens (radiation, chemicals). May be harmful, beneficial, or neutral.
Natural Selection
The process where organisms with favourable traits are more likely to survive, reproduce, and pass on their genes. "Survival of the fittest."
Niche
The functional role of an organism in its ecosystem — what it eats, where it lives, when it's active, how it interacts with others.
Nitrification
Conversion of ammonium (NH₄⁺) to nitrites and then nitrates (NO₃⁻) by nitrifying bacteria in soil. Requires oxygen.
Nitrogen Fixation
Conversion of atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) to ammonium (NH₄⁺) by nitrogen-fixing bacteria (e.g., Rhizobium in root nodules of legumes).
Nucleus
The organelle containing DNA. Controls cell activities. Surrounded by a nuclear membrane with pores.
Osmosis
Movement of water from a region of higher water concentration to lower water concentration across a selectively permeable membrane.
Pathogen
A microorganism that causes disease. Includes bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites.
Phenotype
The observable characteristics of an organism, determined by genotype and environment. E.g., brown eyes.
Photosynthesis
Process by which plants convert light energy to chemical energy. 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂. Occurs in chloroplasts.
Plasmolysis
Shrinkage of cell contents away from the cell wall when a plant cell is placed in a hypertonic solution. Water leaves by osmosis.
Population
All the organisms of the same species living in a particular area at a particular time.
Producer
An organism that makes its own food (autotroph). The first trophic level in a food chain. E.g., plants, algae.
Prokaryotic
A cell without a true nucleus or membrane-bound organelles. Includes bacteria. DNA is a single circular chromosome.
Protein
A macromolecule made of amino acids joined by peptide bonds. Functions: enzymes, antibodies, structural (collagen), transport (haemoglobin).
Recessive
An allele that is only expressed in the phenotype when homozygous (two copies). Represented by a lowercase letter.
Ribosome
The organelle where protein synthesis (translation) occurs. Found free in cytoplasm or attached to rough ER.
Selectively Permeable
A membrane that allows some molecules to pass through but not others. The cell membrane is selectively permeable.
Stomata
Tiny pores on the underside of leaves. Controlled by guard cells. Allow gas exchange (CO₂ in, O₂ out) and transpiration (water vapour out).
Transpiration
Loss of water vapour from the leaves of a plant through the stomata. Driven by evaporation.
Trophic Level
The position an organism occupies in a food chain. T1 = producer, T2 = primary consumer, T3 = secondary consumer, etc.
Turgid
A plant cell that is swollen with water. The vacuole is full and pushes the cell membrane against the cell wall. Keeps plants upright.
Vaccination
Introduction of a weakened or dead pathogen to stimulate the immune system to produce antibodies, providing future immunity.
Xylem
Plant tissue that transports water and minerals upward from roots to leaves. Made of dead, hollow cells with lignin walls.

Key Equations & Formulas - LC Biology

Essential equations, chemical reactions, and formulas you need to know.

Photosynthesis

Overall Equation
6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen (requires light energy and chlorophyll)

Light Stage
Water → H⁺ + e⁻ + O₂ (photolysis)
ADP + P → ATP (photophosphorylation)
NADP + H⁺ → NADPH (reduction)

Occurs on thylakoid membranes. Light splits water, producing ATP and NADPH.

Dark Stage (Calvin Cycle)
CO₂ + RuBP → 2 × GP → G3P → Glucose
Uses ATP and NADPH from the light stage

Occurs in the stroma. CO₂ is fixed into organic molecules.

Respiration

Aerobic Respiration
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 38 ATP

Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy

Glycolysis
Glucose (6C) → 2 Pyruvate (3C) + 2 ATP + 2 NADH

Occurs in cytoplasm. Does not require oxygen.

Anaerobic (Animals)
Glucose → Lactic Acid + 2 ATP

Occurs when oxygen is insufficient (e.g., sprinting).

Anaerobic (Yeast/Fermentation)
Glucose → Ethanol + CO₂ + 2 ATP

Used in brewing (ethanol) and baking (CO₂ makes bread rise).

Genetics

Monohybrid Cross
Parent 1 (Bb) × Parent 2 (Bb)
Offspring: BB : Bb : Bb : bb = 3:1 ratio

Phenotype ratio 3 dominant : 1 recessive. Genotype ratio 1:2:1.

DNA Replication
DNA → (helicase unwinds) → 2 single strands → (DNA polymerase adds complementary bases) → 2 identical DNA molecules

Semi-conservative: each new molecule has one old strand and one new strand.

Protein Synthesis
DNA → (transcription) → mRNA → (translation at ribosome) → Protein
Codon (3 bases on mRNA) codes for 1 amino acid

Ecology Calculations

Percentage Frequency
% Frequency = (Number of quadrats containing species / Total quadrats) × 100
Percentage Cover
% Cover = Estimated area covered by species in quadrat / Total area of quadrat × 100
Population Estimation (Capture-Recapture)
N = (n₁ × n₂) / n₃

N = estimated population, n₁ = first capture, n₂ = second capture, n₃ = recaptured marked individuals

Energy Transfer
Only ~10% of energy is transferred between trophic levels
The rest is lost as heat (respiration), waste, and uneaten parts

Enzymes & Digestion

Enzyme Action
Substrate + Enzyme → Enzyme-Substrate Complex → Products + Enzyme

Enzyme is unchanged and can be reused.

Key Digestive Enzymes
Amylase: Starch → Maltose (mouth, small intestine)
Protease/Pepsin: Protein → Amino acids (stomach, small intestine)
Lipase: Fats → Fatty acids + Glycerol (small intestine)
Optimum Conditions
Most enzymes: ~37°C, pH 7 (neutral)
Pepsin (stomach): pH 2 (acidic)
Above optimum → denaturation

Food Tests

Starch Test
Add iodine solution → Blue-black = starch present
Reducing Sugar (Benedict's)
Add Benedict's reagent + heat → Blue → Green → Orange → Red = sugar present
Protein (Biuret)
Add NaOH + CuSO₄ → Purple/violet = protein present
Fat (Emulsion Test)
Shake with ethanol, pour into water → White emulsion = fat present
Vitamin C (DCPIP)
Add DCPIP (blue dye) to juice → Decolourises = vitamin C present

Q1: Nutrition - Section A 20 marks

Answer any 5 from parts (a) to (f). Each part worth 4 marks.

(a) General formula for carbohydrates 4

Answer: Cx(H2O)y
Why? Carbohydrates contain carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. The ratio of H:O is always 2:1 (like water). Examples: glucose C₆H₁₂O₆ fits the pattern C₆(H₂O)₆.

(b) Four chemical elements in all proteins 4

Answer: Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N)
Remember CHON. All proteins contain these four elements. Some also contain sulfur (S) in amino acids like cysteine.

(c) One structural role of proteins 4

Answer: Muscles / tendons / ligaments / hair / skin / nails (any one)
Structural vs functional. Structural proteins provide support and shape (collagen, keratin). Functional proteins include enzymes, antibodies, hormones.

(d) Small subunits that make protein 4

Answer: Amino acids
20 different amino acids can combine in different sequences to make thousands of different proteins. They link via peptide bonds.

(e) One water-soluble vitamin 4

Answer: Vitamin B (or B1, B2, B12 etc.) / Vitamin C
Water-soluble vs fat-soluble. Water-soluble: B and C (not stored, need daily intake). Fat-soluble: A, D, E, K (stored in body fat).

(f) One trace element found in food 4

Answer: Copper (Cu) / Iron (Fe) / Zinc (Zn) / Iodine (I)
Trace elements are needed in tiny amounts. Iron is in haemoglobin. Iodine is needed for thyroid hormones. Zinc aids enzyme function.

Q2: Plant Root Structure - Section A 20 marks

Diagram shows a longitudinal section through a plant root with structures X, Y, Z labelled.

(a) Structure X and its function 6

Name: Root hair [3]
Function: Absorption of water or minerals [3]
Root hairs are extensions of epidermal cells. They increase surface area for absorption from soil. Short-lived (few weeks).

(b) Tissue type Y and its function 6

Name: Vascular tissue [3]
Function: Transport of water / minerals / food [3]
Vascular tissue includes xylem (water & minerals up) and phloem (sugars up/down). Forms the central core in roots.

(c) Tissue Z where rapid mitosis occurs 3

Answer: Meristematic (tissue)
Meristem = region of rapid cell division. Found at root tips and shoot tips. Cells are small, thin-walled, actively dividing.

(d) Draw transverse section at dashed line 5

Drawing: Circle with vascular tissue in centre [3]
Labels: Any two from: dermal tissue, vascular tissue, ground tissue, root hair [2×1]
Cross-section shows: Outer epidermis (dermal), cortex (ground tissue), central vascular cylinder. Different from stem where vascular bundles are arranged in a ring.

Q3: Aerobic & Anaerobic Respiration - Section A 20 marks

Diagram shows a mitochondrion where stage 2 of aerobic respiration occurs.

(a) Name the cell organelle shown 2

Answer: Mitochondrion
Mitochondria (plural) are the "powerhouses" of the cell. They have a double membrane - cristae (inner folds) increase surface area for ATP production.

(b) Name the cycle in stage 2 3

Answer: Krebs cycle
Stage 1 = glycolysis (cytosol). Stage 2 = Krebs cycle (mitochondria). Krebs cycle produces CO₂, NADH, FADH₂, and some ATP.

(c) What does ATP stand for? 3

Answer: Adenosine triphosphate
ATP is the energy currency of cells. When the third phosphate bond breaks (ATP → ADP + P), energy is released for cellular work.

(d) Function of NAD⁺ 3

Answer: Carries (high energy) electrons and protons
NAD⁺ is an electron carrier. It accepts electrons (becomes NADH), then delivers them to the electron transport chain where ATP is made.

(e) Condition for anaerobic respiration 3

Answer: Lack of oxygen / described
When oxygen runs out (intense exercise, waterlogged soil), cells switch to anaerobic respiration. Much less efficient - only 2 ATP per glucose vs 38 ATP aerobically.

(f) Where anaerobic respiration occurs 3

Answer: Cytosol
Anaerobic = no mitochondria needed. Occurs in cytosol (cell fluid). Just glycolysis, no Krebs cycle or electron transport.

(g) One main product of anaerobic respiration 3

Answer: Lactic acid (animals) / Ethanol (yeast, plants)
In muscle cells: glucose → lactic acid (causes muscle fatigue). In yeast: glucose → ethanol + CO₂ (brewing, baking).

Q4: Cell Membrane & Structure - Section A 20 marks

Diagram shows cell membrane ultrastructure with parts X and Y labelled.

(a) Name parts X and Y 4

X: Phospholipid [2]
Y: Protein [2]
Fluid mosaic model: Phospholipid bilayer (double layer) with proteins embedded. Phospholipids have hydrophilic heads (out) and hydrophobic tails (in).

(b) Function of cell membrane 3

Answer: Controls what substances pass through / selective permeability
Semi-permeable membrane allows some substances through (water, small molecules) but blocks others (large molecules, ions without channels).

(c)(i) Term for cells without membrane-bound organelles 3

Answer: Prokaryotic
Prokaryotes = bacteria. No nucleus, no mitochondria, no chloroplasts. DNA free in cytoplasm. Small and simple.

(c)(ii) Term for cells with membrane-bound organelles 3

Answer: Eukaryotic
Eukaryotes = animals, plants, fungi, protists. Have nucleus, mitochondria, and other organelles. Larger and more complex than prokaryotes.

(d)(i) Sketch plant cell - label membrane & wall 5

Sketch: Showing cell membrane inside cell wall [3]
Labels: Cell membrane [1], Cell wall [1]
Plant cells have BOTH. Cell wall (outer, rigid, cellulose) + cell membrane (inner, controls entry). Animal cells only have membrane.

(d)(ii) One function of plant cell wall 2

Answer: Gives shape (to the cell) / Protection
Cell wall prevents cell bursting when water enters by osmosis. Made of cellulose. Fully permeable (not selective like membrane).

Q5: Bioprocessing & Growth Curves - Section A 20 marks

Image shows equipment for food processing with bacteria. Graph shows population growth curve with stages X and Y.

(a) Common name for equipment 3

Answer: Bioreactor
Bioreactor (fermenter) provides controlled conditions for microorganism growth. Used in brewing, antibiotic production, yogurt making.

(b) Two factors controlled (not nutrients) 6

Answer: Any two from: pH / temperature / pressure / oxygen / stirring rate [2×3]
Optimum conditions maximize growth and product yield. Too hot = enzymes denature. Wrong pH = enzymes don't work efficiently.

(c) Name stages X and Y 6

Stage X: Log (phase) [3]
Stage Y: Stationary (phase) [3]
Growth phases: Lag (adapting) → Log (rapid exponential growth) → Stationary (birth rate = death rate) → Death (toxins accumulate, nutrients depleted).

(d) Type of food processing technique 3

Answer: Continuous (culture)
Continuous vs batch. Continuous = fresh nutrients added constantly, population stays steady. Batch = sealed vessel, population rises then falls.

(e) Draw how line continues if nutrients run out 2

Answer: Line drops (death phase)
No nutrients = death phase. Bacteria die faster than they reproduce. Population crashes. Toxins accumulate.

Q6: Human Digestive System - Section A 20 marks

Diagram shows digestive system with parts A and B labelled.

(a) Name parts A and B 4

A: Oesophagus [2]
B: Stomach [2]
Oesophagus = food pipe connecting mouth to stomach. Stomach = muscular bag where protein digestion begins.

(b) Method food travels through A 6

Name: Peristalsis [3]
Description: (Involuntary) muscular contractions (that push food) [3]
Peristalsis = wave-like muscle contractions. Circular muscles contract behind food bolus, longitudinal muscles contract ahead. Works even upside down.

(c) pH change from A to B 2

Answer: Lowers (becomes more acidic)
Oesophagus ≈ neutral pH 7. Stomach ≈ pH 2 (very acidic). HCl acid kills bacteria and activates pepsin enzyme.

(d) Pancreatic enzyme that digests lipids 3

Answer: Lipase
Lipase breaks down lipids (fats) into fatty acids and glycerol. Produced in pancreas, works in small intestine (alkaline conditions).

(e) Draw and label internal structure of villus 5

Drawing: Villus / lacteal / blood vessels - any two [3]
Labels: Villus / lacteal / capillary - any two [2×1]
Villus = finger-like projection in small intestine. Contains blood capillaries (absorb glucose, amino acids) and lacteal (absorbs fats). Huge surface area for absorption.

Q7: Scientific Method - Section A 20 marks

(a) Testable statement is called 2

Answer: Hypothesis
Hypothesis = educated guess that can be tested by experiment. Must be falsifiable (can be proven wrong).

(b) How to test the statement 3

Answer: Conducting an experiment
Experiment tests hypothesis by changing one variable (independent) and measuring effect on another (dependent). All other variables kept constant.

(c) Function of scientific control 3

Answer: A comparison to the experiment / baseline
Control = identical setup without the variable being tested. Lets you compare and see if your variable caused the effect.

(d) Two limitations of scientific method 6

Answer: Any two from: Accidental discovery / application to nature / (human) bias / human error / constrained by existing knowledge / constrained by technology / ability to interpret results / experimental design [2×3]
Not all discoveries follow method. Penicillin was accidental. Some things can't be tested ethically. Observer bias affects results.

(e) Where biologists publish results 3

Answer: Scientific journal
Peer-reviewed journals ensure quality. Other scientists check the work before publication. Examples: Nature, Science, Cell.

(f) What is a theory? 3

Answer: Supported (or tested) hypothesis
Theory ≠ guess. A theory is a hypothesis tested many times with consistent results. Example: Cell theory, Evolution theory.

Q8: Ecosystem Study (Mandatory Experiment) - Section B 30 marks

(a)(i) What is an ecosystem? 2

Answer: Organisms and their environment
Ecosystem = all living organisms (biotic) and non-living factors (abiotic) interacting in an area. Examples: woodland, pond, rocky shore.

(a)(ii) What aids identification of organisms? 4

Answer: Key (identification key / dichotomous key)
Keys use paired statements to identify species. Each choice narrows down possibilities until species is identified.

(b)(i) Quantitative study of named plant species 12

Named plant species [3]
Equipment: Used quadrat or line transect or belt transect and correct use [3]
Method: Counted and repeated [3]
Calculation: By percentage cover or percentage frequency [3]
Quadrat method: Throw 50cm × 50cm frame randomly. Count how many squares contain the plant. Repeat 10+ times. Calculate % frequency = (squares with plant ÷ total squares) × 100.

(b)(ii) Two abiotic factors and how measured 12

Factor 1: Correctly named (e.g., light intensity) [3]
Method 1: Matching method (e.g., light meter) [3]
Factor 2: Correctly named (e.g., soil pH) [3]
Method 2: Matching method (e.g., pH meter or indicator) [3]
Common abiotic factors: Light (light meter), Temperature (thermometer), Soil pH (pH meter/indicator), Soil moisture (moisture meter), Wind speed (anemometer).

Q11: Plant Reproduction - Section C 60 marks

(a)(i) What is geotropism? 3

Answer: Growth response (of a plant) to gravity
Tropism = directional growth response to stimulus. Geo = earth/gravity. Roots show positive geotropism (grow down). Shoots show negative geotropism (grow up).

(a)(ii) Part that responds positively to geotropism 3

Answer: Root
Positive = grows toward stimulus. Roots grow toward gravity (downward). Ensures roots anchor plant and reach water.

(a)(iii) How does this benefit plants? 3

Answer: Obtain (more) water or minerals or anchorage
Survival advantage. Downward-growing roots find water underground and anchor plant securely.

(b)(i) Collective names for reproductive parts 6

1. Male parts: Stamen [3]
2. Female parts: Carpel [3]
Stamen = anther (makes pollen) + filament. Carpel = stigma + style + ovary.

(b)(ii) Part that holds embryo sac 3

Answer: Ovule (accept ovary)
Ovule is inside the ovary. After fertilisation, ovule becomes seed and ovary becomes fruit.

(b)(iii) Embryo sac develops from this cell 3

Answer: Megaspore mother (cell)
Megaspore mother cell is diploid. Undergoes meiosis to produce 4 haploid cells. 3 degenerate, 1 survives and divides by mitosis to form 8-nuclei embryo sac.

(b)(iv) Parts P and Q involved in double fertilisation 6

P: Polar nucleus [3]
Q: Egg (cell) [3]
Double fertilisation is unique to flowering plants. One sperm fuses with egg (→ zygote). Second sperm fuses with 2 polar nuclei (→ endosperm).

(b)(v) Why P and Q genetically identical 3

Answer: They were produced by mitosis
Mitosis produces genetically identical cells. All 8 nuclei in embryo sac came from mitosis of one megaspore.

(b)(vi) What P and Q develop into after fertilisation 6

P: Endosperm [3]
Q: Diploid zygote [3]
Endosperm = food store for embryo (triploid 3n). Zygote divides to form embryo (diploid 2n).

(c)(i) Two methods of seed dispersal 6

Answer: Any two from: Wind / animal / water / self (explosive) [2×3]
Examples: Wind (dandelion, sycamore), Animal (berries, burrs), Water (coconut), Explosive (gorse pod).

(c)(ii) Two advantages of seed dispersal 6

Answer: Any two from: Reduce competition / colonise new habitats / better chance of survival [2×3]
Why disperse? Seedlings under parent plant compete for light, water, nutrients. Spreading increases species range.

(c)(iii) Two advantages of dormancy 6

Answer: Any two from: Allows time for development of embryo / allows time for dispersal / allows survival in unfavourable conditions [2×3]
Dormancy = period when seed doesn't germinate even with water/warmth. Lets it survive winter or drought.

(c)(iv) Examples of asexual reproduction 6

1. Using leaves: Correct example (e.g., Bryophyllum) [3]
2. Using buds: Correct example (e.g., potato, daffodil) [3]
Vegetative propagation = asexual reproduction using plant parts. Leaves (Kalanchoe), Stems (runners in strawberry), Roots (dahlia), Buds (potato tuber).

Q12: Genetics & DNA - Section C 60 marks

(a)(i) Explain mutation 3

Answer: Change in the structure (or sequence) of DNA (or of a chromosome)
Mutation = permanent change in genetic material. Can be beneficial, harmful, or neutral. Source of genetic variation.

(a)(ii) Two causes of mutations 6

Answer: Any two from: Radiation / chemicals / viruses [2×3]
Mutagens increase mutation rate. UV radiation, X-rays, tobacco smoke, certain viruses (HPV). Most mutations during DNA replication are random errors.

(b)(i) Distinguish gene and allele 6

Gene: Region of chromosome (or DNA) that contains (genetic) code for (production of) a protein [3]
Allele: Form of a gene [3]
Example: Gene for eye colour has different alleles (brown, blue, green). You inherit one allele from each parent.

(b)(ii)1. Genotype ebony/normal heterozygous 3

Answer: EeNn
Heterozygous = two different alleles. E (ebony dominant) and e (black recessive), N (normal dominant) and n (curly recessive).

(b)(ii)2. Genotype black body/curly wings 3

Answer: eenn
Recessive phenotype = homozygous recessive genotype. Black is recessive (ee), curly is recessive (nn).

(b)(ii)3. Offspring genotypes and phenotypes 12

Cross: EeNn × eenn
EeNn: ebony, normal [3]
Eenn: ebony, curly [3]
eeNn: black, normal [3]
eenn: black, curly [3]
Use Punnett square. EeNn produces gametes: EN, En, eN, en. eenn produces only en. 4 different offspring types in 1:1:1:1 ratio.

(b)(iii) Term for genes on same chromosome 3

Answer: Linked
Linked genes are inherited together (don't follow Mendel's law of independent assortment). Closer together = less likely to separate by crossing over.

(c)(i) What does DNA stand for? 3

Answer: Deoxyribonucleic acid
DNA carries genetic information. Double helix structure discovered by Watson & Crick (1953).

(c)(ii) Purines and pyrimidines 6

Purines: Adenine (A) and Guanine (G) [3]
Pyrimidines: Cytosine (C) and Thymine (T) [3]
Base pairing rules: A pairs with T (2 H-bonds), G pairs with C (3 H-bonds). Purines are larger (double ring), pyrimidines smaller (single ring).

(c)(iii) One difference between DNA and RNA 3

Answer: DNA is double stranded, RNA is single stranded
OR DNA has deoxyribose sugar, RNA has ribose
OR DNA has thymine, RNA has uracil
RNA (ribonucleic acid) is temporary copy of DNA used to make proteins. mRNA, tRNA, rRNA are different types.

(c)(iv) Four steps in DNA profiling 12

1. Cells are broken down to release DNA [3]
2. DNA is cut into fragments using enzymes [3]
3. Fragments are separated based on size [3]
4. Pattern of fragments is analysed [3]
DNA profiling uses gel electrophoresis to separate fragments. Smaller fragments travel further. Pattern is unique to each individual (except identical twins). Used in forensics, paternity testing.
