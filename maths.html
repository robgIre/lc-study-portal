Top Exam Tips

1. Show ALL working! Marks are awarded for METHOD. Even if your final answer is wrong, you get marks for each correct step.
2. Use the Formulae and Tables booklet. You get this in the exam. It contains log tables, trigonometric values, formulas, and statistical tables.
3. Don't round too early. Keep full accuracy until the final answer. Incorrect rounding loses marks.
4. Write formulas before calculating. Writing the formula earns marks even if the calculation goes wrong.
5. Check units! Give answers in simplest form where relevant. Missing units can lose marks.
6. Choose your questions wisely. You only need 5 from Section A and 3 from Section B. Pick your strongest topics.
7. Answer without sufficient work earns minimum marks. Even if correct, you need to show the steps.

Algebra

Quadratic Formula

For ax² + bx + c = 0

Δ = b² - 4ac

Δ > 0: two real roots | Δ = 0: one repeated root | Δ < 0: no real roots

Sum and Product of Roots
α + β = -b/a | αβ = c/a
Completing the Square
ax² + bx + c = a(x + h)² + k

where h = b/(2a), k = c - ah²

log(AB) = log A + log B
log(A/B) = log A - log B
log(A^n) = n log A
log_a(a) = 1 | log_a(1) = 0

Maths Glossary

Search for any term. All definitions written in plain language.

Absolute Value
The distance of a number from zero on the number line, always positive. Written as |x|. Example: |-5| = 5.
Arithmetic Sequence
A sequence where each term is found by adding a constant (common difference) to the previous term. Example: 3, 7, 11, 15... (d = 4).
Geometric Sequence
A sequence where each term is found by multiplying the previous term by a constant (common ratio). Example: 2, 6, 18, 54... (r = 3).
Binomial Expansion
Expanding expressions of the form (a + b)^n using the binomial theorem. Uses combinations C(n,r) to find coefficients.
Chain Rule
A method for differentiating composite functions. If y = f(g(x)), then dy/dx = f'(g(x)) × g'(x).
Quotient Rule
A method for differentiating fractions. If y = u/v, then dy/dx = [v(du/dx) - u(dv/dx)] / v².
Product Rule
A method for differentiating products. If y = uv, then dy/dx = u(dv/dx) + v(du/dx).
Derivative
The rate of change of a function with respect to a variable. Represents the slope of the tangent line at any point. Written as f'(x) or dy/dx.
Integration
The reverse process of differentiation. Used to find areas under curves, volumes, and accumulated quantities. The integral of f(x) is written ∫f(x)dx.
Definite Integral
Integration with limits. ∫[a to b] f(x)dx gives the net area between the curve and the x-axis from x = a to x = b.
Complex Number
A number of the form a + bi where i² = -1. Has a real part (a) and an imaginary part (b). Example: 3 + 4i.
Modulus
The absolute value of a complex number. For z = a + bi, |z| = √(a² + b²). Represents distance from the origin in the complex plane.
Argument
The angle a complex number makes with the positive real axis. For z = a + bi, arg(z) = tan⁻¹(b/a).
De Moivre's Theorem
States that [r(cos θ + i sin θ)]^n = r^n(cos nθ + i sin nθ). Used to find powers and roots of complex numbers.
Polar Form
Expressing a complex number as z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) where r is the modulus and θ is the argument.
Discriminant
The part b² - 4ac of the quadratic formula. Tells how many real solutions a quadratic equation has. Positive = 2 roots, zero = 1 root, negative = 0 real roots.
Completing the Square
Rewriting a quadratic expression ax² + bx + c in the form a(x + h)² + k. Useful for finding the vertex of a parabola and solving equations.
Conjugate
For a + bi, the conjugate is a - bi. For complex numbers, multiplying by the conjugate gives a real number. Used to simplify complex fractions.
Slope
The steepness of a line. Calculated as rise/run or (y₂ - y₁)/(x₂ - x₁). Also called gradient.
Intercept
Where a line crosses an axis. y-intercept is where x = 0. x-intercept is where y = 0.
Perpendicular
Two lines meeting at 90°. If one line has slope m, a perpendicular line has slope -1/m.
Tangent (to a curve)
A line that touches a curve at exactly one point. The slope of the tangent equals the derivative at that point.
Sine Rule
In any triangle: a/sin A = b/sin B = c/sin C. Used when you know two angles and one side, or two sides and a non-included angle.
Cosine Rule
In any triangle: c² = a² + b² - 2ab cos C. Used when you know three sides or two sides and the included angle.
Radian
A unit for measuring angles. 2π radians = 360°. 1 radian ≈ 57.3°. Used in calculus because it simplifies formulas.
Period
The interval after which a function repeats. For sin x and cos x, the period is 2π. For tan x, the period is π.
Amplitude
For y = A sin(x), the amplitude is |A|. It's half the distance between maximum and minimum values.
Pythagoras' Theorem
In a right-angled triangle: a² + b² = c² where c is the hypotenuse. Extended to 3D: d² = x² + y² + z².
Congruent
Two shapes that are exactly the same size and shape. They may be rotated or reflected but have identical dimensions.
Similar
Two shapes with the same angles but different sizes. All corresponding lengths are in the same ratio (scale factor).
Scale Factor
The ratio by which a shape is enlarged. Lengths scale by k, areas by k², volumes by k³.
Probability
A measure of how likely an event is to occur. Ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain). P(event) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes.
Independent Events
Two events where the outcome of one doesn't affect the other. For independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B).
Conditional Probability
The probability of an event given that another event has occurred. Written P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B).
Mutually Exclusive
Events that cannot happen at the same time. If A and B are mutually exclusive, P(A and B) = 0.
Mean
The average of a set of numbers. Add all values and divide by how many there are.
Median
The middle value when data is arranged in order. For even number of values, it's the average of the two middle values.
Mode
The value that appears most frequently in a dataset.
Quartiles
Values that divide data into four equal parts. Q₁ = 25th percentile, Q₂ = median, Q₃ = 75th percentile.
Interquartile Range (IQR)
The range of the middle 50% of data. IQR = Q₃ - Q₁. A measure of spread that's resistant to outliers.
Standard Deviation
A measure of how spread out numbers are from the mean. Larger standard deviation = more spread out data.
Normal Distribution
A bell-shaped probability distribution. Many natural phenomena follow this pattern. Symmetric around the mean.
Z-Score
The number of standard deviations a value is from the mean. Z = (X - μ) / σ. Used to standardize normal distributions.
Confidence Interval
A range of values likely to contain the true population parameter. A 95% confidence interval means we're 95% confident the true value is in this range.
Hypothesis Test
A statistical method to test a claim about a population. Uses a null hypothesis (H₀) and alternative hypothesis (H₁).
p-value
The probability of getting results as extreme as observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true. If p-value < 0.05, we reject H₀ at 5% significance level.
Stratified Sample
A sampling method where the population is divided into groups (strata) and random samples are taken from each group proportionally.
Expected Value
The average outcome if an experiment is repeated many times. E(X) = Σ[x × P(x)].
Combination
The number of ways to choose r items from n items where order doesn't matter. C(n,r) = n! / [r!(n-r)!].
Permutation
The number of ways to arrange r items from n items where order matters. P(n,r) = n! / (n-r)!.
Limit
The value a function approaches as the input approaches some value. Written as lim(x→a) f(x).
Proof by Induction
A method of mathematical proof. Step 1: Prove base case (n=1). Step 2: Assume true for n=k. Step 3: Prove true for n=k+1. Conclusion: True for all natural numbers.

Mark Scheme Decoder

Understanding how Maths examiners award marks is crucial for maximising your score.

General Marking Principles

PrincipleDetail
Step-based markingMost questions are marked in steps. Each step can be independently correct or incorrect.
Show your workAn answer without sufficient supporting work earns only the lowest level of credit (typically Partial Credit or Low Partial Credit).
Rounding penaltyIncorrect rounding is marked with a * and loses 1 mark if you would otherwise have full credit. Only applied once per unit of marking.
Unit penaltiesMissing or incorrect units may lose marks where specified (typically -1).
Multiple answersIf you give more than the required number of answers without crossing out, the lowest mark is awarded.
Accept candidate's workIf you make an error in part (a), you can still get full marks in part (b) by correctly using your wrong answer from (a) - unless it oversimplifies the work.

Golden Rules for Maximum Marks

1. Write the formula first. Even if your calculation is wrong, the formula earns marks.
2. Show EVERY step. Each step is worth marks. Don't skip steps in your head.
3. Label your work. Make it clear which part you're answering: (a)(i), (a)(ii), etc.
4. Don't round until the end. Keep full calculator accuracy throughout. Only round the final answer.
5. Accept your own errors. If you make a mistake in part (a), use that wrong answer in part (b). You'll still get marks for correct method in (b).
6. Cross out unwanted attempts. If you try multiple methods, cross out the one you don't want marked. Otherwise the lowest mark applies.
7. Use the Formulae and Tables booklet. You don't need to memorize everything - use the booklet provided.
8. Check your units. Include units in final answers where relevant (m, cm², radians, etc.).
9. Simplify where asked. If the question says "simplify", "give in surd form", or "exact value", don't use decimals.

Paper 1 - Q1: Algebra 30 marks

(a) Solve the inequality |x - 3| ≤ 12 10

Question: Solve for x ∈ ℝ

Method 1: Split the absolute value
|x - 3| ≤ 12 means:
x - 3 ≤ 12 AND x - 3 ≥ -12
x ≤ 15 AND x ≥ -9
Answer: -9 ≤ x ≤ 15
Why this works: The absolute value |x - 3| represents the distance from x to 3. If this distance is ≤ 12, then x can be 12 units to the left (3 - 12 = -9) or 12 units to the right (3 + 12 = 15) of 3.

(b) Multiply out and simplify: (4x - 10√x)(2x + 5√x - 7) 10

Distribute each term:
= 4x(2x + 5√x - 7) - 10√x(2x + 5√x - 7)
= 8x² + 20x√x - 28x - 20x√x - 50x + 70√x
= 8x² + (20x√x - 20x√x) + (-28x - 50x) + 70√x
= 8x² - 78x + 70√x
Tip: The middle terms 20x√x cancel out. Check you have 9 terms total before simplifying (6 in the expanded form, 3 in the final answer).

(c) Find three solutions to 4x³ - 12x² - 7x + 30 = 0 10

Given: (2x + 3) is a factor

Use long division or synthetic division:
4x³ - 12x² - 7x + 30 = (2x + 3)(2x² - 9x + 10)

Now factor the quadratic:
2x² - 9x + 10 = (2x - 5)(x - 2)

So the full factorization is:
(2x + 3)(2x - 5)(x - 2) = 0

Solutions:
2x + 3 = 0 → x = -3/2
2x - 5 = 0 → x = 5/2
x - 2 = 0 → x = 2

Answer: x = -3/2, x = 5/2, x = 2
Steps earn marks: Setting up the division (2), finding first term (2), finding the quadratic (2), factorizing it (2), finding all 3 values (2) = 10 marks total.

Paper 1 - Q2: Calculus and Functions 30 marks

(a)(i) Find f'(x) where f(x) = 6 + x² + sin 4x 5

Differentiate term by term:
f'(x) = 0 + 2x + 4cos 4x
f'(x) = 2x + 4cos 4x
Chain rule: The derivative of sin 4x is cos 4x × 4 (derivative of inside function).

(a)(ii) Find the equation of the tangent at x = 0 in form ax + by + c = 0 10

Step 1: Find the slope at x = 0
f'(0) = 2(0) + 4cos(4×0) = 4cos(0) = 4
Slope m = 4

Step 2: Find the y-value at x = 0
f(0) = 6 + 0² + sin(4×0) = 6
Point of contact: (0, 6)

Step 3: Use point-slope form
y - 6 = 4(x - 0)
y - 6 = 4x

Step 4: Rearrange to required form
4x - y + 6 = 0 OR -4x + y - 6 = 0
Answer: 4x - y + 6 = 0
Marks breakdown: Finding slope (2-3), finding y-value (2-3), substituting into line formula (2-3), converting to required form (2-3). Total = 10 marks.

(b)(i) For g(x) shown in the graph, find values of x where g'(x) > 2 5

Graph shows: g(x) is made of two line segments from x = 0 to x = 4

g'(x) represents the slope of g(x)
From the graph: the second line segment (from x = 3 to x = 4) has slope > 2
Answer: 3 < x ≤ 4
Understanding g'(x): The derivative is the slope. Where the graph is steeper (larger slope), g'(x) is larger.

(b)(ii) Find g(g(3)) 5

From the graph:
g(3) = 1
g(g(3)) = g(1) = 1/3
Answer: 1/3
Composite functions: Work from the inside out. First find g(3), then use that result to find g(1).

(b)(iii) Draw the graph of y = g⁻¹(x) 5

The inverse function g⁻¹(x) is the reflection of g(x) in the line y = x

(b) Use de Moivre's theorem to prove cos 2θ = cos²θ - sin²θ 10

By de Moivre's theorem:
(cos θ + i sin θ)² = cos 2θ + i sin 2θ

Expand the left side:
LHS = (cos θ)² + 2i cos θ sin θ + (i sin θ)²
= cos²θ + 2i cos θ sin θ + i²sin²θ
= cos²θ + 2i cos θ sin θ - sin²θ
= (cos²θ - sin²θ) + i(2 cos θ sin θ)

Equate real parts:
Real part of LHS = cos²θ - sin²θ
Real part of RHS = cos 2θ

Therefore: cos 2θ = cos²θ - sin²θ ✓
Key insight: De Moivre's theorem connects powers of complex numbers to trigonometric identities. By equating real and imaginary parts, we derive double-angle formulas.

Paper 2 - Q10: Statistics with Normal Distribution 50 marks

Context: Test scores worldwide are normally distributed with mean 400, standard deviation 60.

(a)(i) Fill in missing values using empirical rule 10

Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7): 68% within μ ± σ, 95% within μ ± 2σ, 99.7% within μ ± 3σ

Given: Mean = 400, SD = 60
One value shown: 520 (which is 400 + 2×60)

Missing values at μ ± σ:
400 - 60 = 340
400 + 60 = 460

Missing value at μ - 2σ:
400 - 2(60) = 280

The four missing values are: 280, 340, 460, 520

(a)(ii) Proportion scoring above 420 10

Standardize using z-score:
z = (X - μ)/σ = (420 - 400)/60 = 20/60 = 1/3 ≈ 0.33

From normal distribution table:
P(Z < 0.33) ≈ 0.6293
P(Z > 0.33) = 1 - 0.6293 = 0.3707

Answer: 0.37 (to 2 decimal places)

(b) 95% confidence interval for Country X 10

Given: Sample mean = 387, SD = 66.2, n = 2161

95% CI formula:
x̄ ± 1.96(σ/√n)

= 387 ± 1.96(66.2/√2161)
= 387 ± 1.96(66.2/46.49)
= 387 ± 1.96(1.424)
= 387 ± 2.79

Lower bound: 387 - 2.8 = 384.2
Upper bound: 387 + 2.8 = 389.8

Answer: (384.2, 389.8)

(c)(i) Test statistic for Country Y 10

Given: x̄ = 403, σ = 70.6, n = 2724, μ₀ = 400

z-score formula:
z = (x̄ - μ₀)/(σ/√n)

z = (403 - 400)/(70.6/√2724)
z = 3/(70.6/52.19)

z ≈ 2.22

(c)(ii) p-value and conclusion 10

Two-tailed test (H₁: μ ≠ 400)
z = 2.22

P(Z > 2.22) ≈ 0.0132

p-value ≈ 0.026

Since p-value (0.026) < 0.05 (5% significance level), we reject the null hypothesis.
There is sufficient evidence to conclude that the mean score for Country Y is different from 400.

(d) Stratified sampling with respect to having a pet 10

How to take stratified sample:
Divide the 2520 students into two strata:
- Stratum 1: Students with a pet (50% = 1260 students)
- Stratum 2: Students without a pet (50% = 1260 students)
Take random samples proportionally from each stratum.

Why this would NOT be useful:
Having a pet in the home is unlikely to be related to maths test scores. Stratifying on an irrelevant variable doesn't reduce sampling error and adds unnecessary complexity. Better to use simple random sampling or stratify on factors that might affect maths performance (e.g., school type, prior maths grades).
