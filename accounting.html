Time Management - The Golden Rule

You get 180 minutes for 400 marks. That means:

1 mark = 27 seconds | 10 marks = 4.5 minutes

Never spend more time on a question than its marks justify. If you're stuck on a 4-mark working, move on after 2 minutes.

Top Exam Tips

1. Always show workings! Even if your final answer is wrong, you get marks for correct workings. The marking scheme awards marks at each step, not just the final figure. A wrong answer with good workings might get 5 out of 7 marks. A wrong answer with no workings gets 0.
2. Use proper headings. Write "Trading Profit & Loss Account for [name] for year ended 31/12/2024" - this heading alone is worth [1] mark.
3. Label everything with euro signs. Column headings should show the euro symbol. This is a presentation mark.
4. Attempt every question you've chosen. Even partial answers score marks. A half-finished balance sheet still earns marks for the lines you completed.
5. Don't do Q1(A) AND Q1(B). You choose ONE. If you answer both, only one will be marked.
6. Carry forward errors. If your Sales figure is wrong but you correctly use it throughout, you still earn marks for correct method. Examiners call this "own figure" marking.
7. Theory questions are free marks. Parts (b) and (c) of questions often ask for explanations. Learn the key phrases - these marks require no calculations.

Mark-up on Cost
Profit / Cost x 100

If mark-up is 25%, selling price = cost x 1.25

Margin on Sales
Profit / Selling Price x 100

Depreciation

(Cost - Residual Value) / Useful Life

OR: Cost x Rate%

Same amount each year. Most common in LC exams.

Reducing Balance Method
Net Book Value x Rate%

Amount decreases each year as NBV falls.

Net Book Value (NBV)
Cost - Accumulated Depreciation

Interpretation of Accounts (Ratios)

Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)
(Operating Profit + Investment Income) / Capital Employed x 100

Measures how efficiently a company uses its capital. Higher is better. Compare to risk-free rate (0-3%).

Earnings Per Share (EPS)
(Net Profit - Preference Dividends) / Number of Ordinary Shares
Dividend Per Share (DPS)
Total Ordinary Dividends / Number of Ordinary Shares
Price Earnings Ratio (P/E)
Market Price per Share / EPS

How many years to recoup investment at current earnings. Lower = better value.

Dividend Cover
(Net Profit - Preference Dividend) / Ordinary Dividend

How many times the company can pay its dividend from profits. Higher = safer.

Dividend Yield
DPS / Market Price x 100

(Operating Profit + Investment Income) / Interest

How many times the company can pay its interest. Higher = less risk.

Acid Test Ratio
(Current Assets - Stock) / Current Liabilities

Ideal is 1:1. Can the company pay its short-term debts without selling stock?

Gearing Ratio
Long-term Debt / (Long-term Debt + Equity) x 100

Below 50% = lowly geared (good). Above 50% = highly geared (risky).

Long-term Debt / Equity x 100
Debtors Collection Period
Debtors / Credit Sales x 12 months (or x 365 days)

Target Profit (units needed)

Accounting Glossary

Search for any term. All definitions are written in plain language.

Trial Balance
A list of all the balances in a business's accounts at a specific date. Debit balances (assets, expenses) go on the left; credit balances (liabilities, income, capital) go on the right. If the two sides are equal, the books balance. It's the starting point for preparing final accounts.
Trading, Profit & Loss Account
A financial statement showing how much money a business made (or lost) during the year. The trading account calculates Gross Profit (Sales minus Cost of Sales). The profit and loss account deducts expenses from Gross Profit to find Net Profit.
Balance Sheet
A snapshot of what a business owns (assets) and owes (liabilities) on a specific date. It must always balance: Assets = Liabilities + Capital. Think of it as a photograph of the business's financial position at year-end.
Depreciation
The reduction in value of a fixed asset over time due to wear and tear, age, or obsolescence. It's an expense in the P&L and reduces the asset's value on the balance sheet. Common methods: Straight Line (same amount each year) and Reducing Balance (decreasing amount).
Provision for Bad Debts (BDP)
An estimate of the amount of money owed to the business (debtors) that may never be paid. It's set as a percentage of debtors. An increase in the provision is an expense; a decrease is treated as income.
Bad Debts
Money owed to the business that will definitely never be collected - the debtor cannot or will not pay. Written off as an expense in the P&L. Different from provision for bad debts, which is an estimate.
Accruals (Expenses Due)
Expenses that have been incurred during the accounting period but haven't been paid yet. Example: electricity used in December but the bill arrives in January. The expense must be included in this year's accounts. Shown as a current liability on the balance sheet.
Prepayments (Expenses Prepaid)
Expenses that have been paid in advance - you've paid for something you haven't fully used yet. Example: insurance paid for 12 months but only 9 months relate to this year. The unused 3 months is a prepayment. Shown as a current asset on the balance sheet.
Debtors (Accounts Receivable)
People or businesses who owe money TO the business, usually because they bought goods on credit. A current asset. Also called "accounts receivable" or "trade receivables".
Creditors (Accounts Payable)
People or businesses the company owes money TO, usually because the business bought goods on credit. A current liability. Also called "accounts payable" or "trade payables".
Capital
The money the owner has invested in the business. On the balance sheet: Opening Capital + Net Profit - Drawings = Closing Capital. For companies, capital comes from issuing shares.
Drawings
Money or goods taken out of the business by the owner for personal use. Reduces capital. Not an expense - it's a reduction of the owner's investment. In a company, the equivalent is dividends.
Fixed Assets (Non-Current Assets)
Items owned by a business for long-term use (more than 1 year), not for resale. Examples: land, buildings, machinery, delivery vans, equipment. Shown on the balance sheet at cost minus depreciation (= Net Book Value).
Current Assets
Items owned by the business that will be converted to cash within 12 months. Examples: stock, debtors, bank balance, cash, prepayments. Listed on the balance sheet in order of liquidity (least liquid first).
Current Liabilities (Creditors due within 1 year)
Debts the business must pay within the next 12 months. Examples: trade creditors, bank overdraft, PAYE/PRSI due, accruals, VAT owed.
Working Capital (Net Current Assets)
Current Assets minus Current Liabilities. Shows whether the business can pay its short-term debts. Positive = healthy. Negative = potential cash flow problems.
Sole Trader
A business owned and run by one person. The owner has unlimited liability (personally responsible for all business debts). Simplest form of business. Produces a Trading P&L Account and Balance Sheet.
Limited Company (Ltd)
A business that is a separate legal entity from its owners (shareholders). Shareholders have limited liability - they can only lose the amount they invested. Has share capital, pays dividends, and must produce published accounts.
Ordinary Shares
The most common type of share. Ordinary shareholders are the owners of the company. They vote at meetings, receive dividends (if declared), but are paid last if the company is wound up. Their dividend is not guaranteed.
Preference Shares
Shares that receive a fixed dividend percentage before ordinary shareholders get anything. They have priority over ordinary shares for dividends and repayment. Usually no voting rights.
Debentures
Long-term loans to a company, usually with a fixed interest rate. Debenture holders are creditors, NOT owners. They receive interest regardless of whether the company makes a profit. Interest is an expense, not a dividend.
Authorised Share Capital
The maximum amount of shares a company is legally allowed to issue, as stated in its Memorandum of Association. Not all authorised shares need to be issued.
Issued Share Capital
The shares that have actually been sold to shareholders. Must be less than or equal to the authorised share capital.
Revaluation Reserve
Created when a fixed asset is revalued (usually upward). The increase in value goes to the revaluation reserve, not to the P&L. It's a capital reserve - cannot be used to pay dividends.
Gearing
The ratio of borrowed money (debt) to equity in a company. Lowly geared (below 50%) = financed mostly by equity = safer. Highly geared (above 50%) = financed mostly by debt = riskier.
ROCE (Return on Capital Employed)
A profitability ratio showing how efficiently a company uses its total capital. Calculated as: (Operating Profit + Investment Income) / Capital Employed x 100. The higher the better. Should exceed the risk-free rate.
Break-Even Point
The level of sales at which a business makes neither a profit nor a loss. Total revenue exactly equals total costs. Calculated as: Fixed Costs / Contribution Per Unit.
Contribution
Selling price minus variable costs. The amount each unit sold "contributes" towards paying off fixed costs and then generating profit. Once total contribution exceeds fixed costs, the business is profitable.
Margin of Safety
The difference between actual/budgeted sales and the break-even point. Shows how much sales can drop before the business starts making a loss. Larger = safer.
Variable Costs
Costs that change in direct proportion to output/sales. More units = higher costs. Examples: raw materials, direct labour, sales commission. Cost per unit stays the same.
Fixed Costs
Costs that stay the same regardless of how many units are produced (within a relevant range). Examples: rent, insurance, salaries of permanent staff. Cost per unit decreases as output increases.
Step Fixed Costs
Costs that are fixed within a certain range of activity but jump to a new level when that range is exceeded. Example: rent is fixed until you need a bigger factory, then it jumps up.
Sensitivity Analysis
A "what if" technique showing how changes in selling price, sales volume, variable costs, or fixed costs affect profit. Helps management plan for different scenarios.
Budget
A financial plan for a future period. It sets targets for income and expenditure. Used to plan, control, and evaluate business performance. Types include production budgets, materials budgets, and cash budgets.
Principal Budget Factor
The factor that limits or constrains a business's activity. Usually sales demand, but could be machine capacity, labour availability, or raw material supply. The budget process starts with this factor.
FIFO (First In, First Out)
A stock valuation method where the oldest stock is assumed to be sold first. Closing stock is valued at the most recent purchase prices. Most commonly used method in Irish accounting.
VAT (Value Added Tax)
A tax on goods and services charged at each stage of production/sale. In Ireland, the standard rate is 23%. Businesses collect VAT on sales and reclaim VAT on purchases. The difference is paid to Revenue.
PAYE, PRSI & USC
Taxes deducted from employees' wages. PAYE = income tax. PRSI = social insurance contributions. USC = Universal Social Charge. The employer deducts these and pays them to Revenue. Shown as a current liability if not yet paid.
Goodwill
An intangible asset representing the difference between the price paid for a business and the fair value of its net assets. Arises when someone pays more for a business than its assets are worth, because of reputation, customer base, etc.
Patent
An intangible fixed asset giving exclusive rights to use an invention for a set period. Written off (amortised) over its useful life. Example: a 6-year patent costs 62,400 - annual write-off = 62,400/6 = 10,400.
Net Worth
The total value of what a person or business owns minus what they owe. Net Worth = Total Assets - Total Liabilities. Used in incomplete records to calculate profit.
Control Account
A summary account in the general ledger that shows the total of all individual accounts in a subsidiary ledger. The Debtors Control Account shows the total owed by all debtors. Used to check accuracy and detect errors.
Suspense Account
A temporary account used when the trial balance doesn't balance. Errors are investigated and corrected, and entries are made to clear the suspense account.
Service Firm
A business that provides services rather than selling goods (e.g., dentist, solicitor, school). Prepares an Income & Expenditure Account instead of a Trading P&L. Uses a Statement of Reserves instead of a Capital Account.
Income & Expenditure Account
The equivalent of a Profit & Loss Account for a service firm or club. Shows income earned and expenses incurred during the year. The surplus (or deficit) is similar to net profit (or loss).
Receipts & Payments Account
A summary of all cash/bank transactions during the year. Similar to a bank statement. Shows actual money in and out - does NOT account for accruals, prepayments, or depreciation. Used as a starting point for service firm accounts.
Tabular Statement
A method of recording transactions by showing the effect on each asset, liability, and equity item in a table format. Each row is a category, each column is a transaction month. All entries must keep the accounting equation in balance.
Consistency Concept
One of the four fundamental accounting concepts. It states that once a business chooses an accounting method (e.g., straight-line depreciation at 2%), it must use the same method every year. If changed, the reason and effect must be disclosed.
Revaluation of Fixed Assets
Adjusting a fixed asset's value on the balance sheet to reflect its current market value. The difference between old and new value goes to the Revaluation Reserve. Important for showing a true and fair view.
Disposal of Fixed Assets
When a fixed asset is sold or scrapped. A Disposal Account is prepared showing: the asset's cost, accumulated depreciation, and sale proceeds. If proceeds exceed NBV = profit on disposal. If less = loss on disposal.
Mortgage
A long-term loan secured against property. The interest is an expense in the P&L. The loan itself is a long-term liability on the balance sheet. If part is due within 12 months, that portion goes under current liabilities.
Dividend
A payment from a company's profits to its shareholders. Preference shareholders get a fixed % first. Ordinary shareholders get whatever the directors recommend. Not an expense - it's a distribution of profit.

Mark Scheme Decoder

Understanding how examiners award marks is the key to maximising your score. Here's how the marking scheme works.

How Marks Are Shown

In the marking scheme, you'll see numbers in square brackets like [4] or [7]. This shows how many marks that specific figure or element is worth.

AnnotationMeaning
[n]Correct element worth n marks
N1, N2...Refer to workings - the calculation behind the figure
[0]No marks - answer incorrect or insufficient
P-Penalty: minus 1 mark for incorrect calculation, misplaced figure, or wrong transfer
Half marksPartial credit for partially correct work (non-transfer)

Where the Big Marks Are

Not all figures are worth the same. Here's what carries the most marks:

Q1A - Sole Trader (120 marks: 75 P&L + 45 Balance Sheet)

Sales [4] - Working: 884,300 + 12,000 = 896,300. Four marks because it involves an adjustment (goods sold on credit with no entry made).
Purchases [7] - Working: 622,400 - 24,800 - 10,750 = 586,850. Seven marks because multiple adjustments: remove van trade-in incorrectly recorded as purchase, and remove goods for own use.
Closing Stock [7] - Working: 84,000 - 2,300 - 10,000 = 71,700. Seven marks for three separate adjustments (damaged stock, goods on credit not dispatched).
Net Profit [5] - The final figure. Worth 5 marks because it proves the whole account is correct. Even if wrong, you get marks for correct workings above.

Key insight: In Q1A, single figures like Purchases [7] and Closing Stock [7] carry more marks than many expense lines. Getting these right is critical.

Q5 - Interpretation of Accounts (100 marks: 50 + 40 + 10)

Cash Sales [12] - Must show formula, substitution, AND correct answer. Marks split across method and accuracy.
ROCE [10] - Formula [2], correct figures [4], correct answer [4]. Even if answer is wrong, stating the correct formula earns 2 marks.
Written Analysis [40] - Part (b) is worth 40 marks for a written report. Marks are allocated per heading: Profitability [7], Liquidity [6], Gearing [7], Dividend Policy [7], Market Value [5], Sector [5], Overall [3]. You MUST use ratios and percentages to support your points.
Gearing Options [10] - Part (c) requires analysing two financing options. Marks for calculations AND implications.

Q8 - Marginal Costing (80 marks)

Break-Even [15] - Must first prepare a marginal cost statement separating variable and fixed costs, then apply the formula. The cost statement itself carries marks.
Each scenario (b-e) [12-14 each] - Each part modifies one variable. Show the formula, substitute correctly, calculate. Full marks require the correct formula AND correct arithmetic.
Theory (f) [12] - Three theory sub-parts worth 12 marks total. Free marks if you know the definitions of sensitivity analysis, step fixed costs, and can sketch a graph.

Golden Rules for Maximum Marks

1. Write the formula first. In ratio questions, writing the correct formula earns marks even if your calculation is wrong.
2. Show every step of workings. For a [7] mark figure, marks are awarded at each step. Three correct steps in a wrong answer could still earn 4-5 marks.
3. Headings earn marks. "Trading Profit & Loss Account for M McConnell for year ended 31/12/2024" = [1] mark just for the heading.
4. Correct labels matter. Writing "Gross Profit" in the right place, "Less Cost of Sales", "Less Expenses" - these structural elements earn marks.
5. Own-figure rule. If you get Sales wrong but correctly carry it through, subsequent figures using your (wrong) Sales figure still earn full marks for method.
6. Theory = guaranteed marks. Parts asking "explain" or "outline" have fixed mark allocations. Memorise key points for each topic.

Q1A: Final Accounts of a Sole Trader 120 marks

You're given a trial balance for M McConnell's business at 31/12/2024, plus 9 adjustments. You must prepare:

  • (a) Trading Profit & Loss Account (75 marks)
  • (b) Balance Sheet (45 marks)

What is a Trial Balance?

Think of it as a summary list. Every transaction in a business is recorded twice (double-entry bookkeeping). The trial balance lists all account balances at year-end. Debit balances (left column) are assets and expenses. Credit balances (right column) are liabilities, income, and capital. The two columns should add up to the same total - that's how you know the books "balance".

But the trial balance figures are often not the final figures. Adjustments are needed for things like depreciation, accruals, prepayments, and errors. That's what the 9 instructions tell you to do.

Step-by-Step Solution

Part (a): Trading Profit & Loss Account

The Trading P&L has three sections: Trading Account (calculating Gross Profit), Expenses (deducted from Gross Profit), and Other Income (added to get Net Profit).

SALES = 896,300 4 marks

Working N1: 884,300 + 12,000 = 896,300
Why? The trial balance shows sales of 884,300. But adjustment (iv) tells us goods were sold on credit for 14,760 (including 23% VAT) with no entry made. We need the sales value EXCLUDING VAT:
14,760 x 100/123 = 12,000 (this is the net sales value)
So we ADD 12,000 to Sales. The VAT portion (2,760) goes to the VAT account, not sales.

OPENING STOCK = 62,500 2 marks

Simple one. Opening stock is always taken directly from the trial balance. No adjustment needed. It's the stock that was on hand at the START of the year (which is the same as last year's closing stock).

PURCHASES = 586,850 7 marks

Working N3: 622,400 - 24,800 - 10,750 = 586,850
Why 3 adjustments?
1. Remove van trade-in (-24,800): Adjustment (iii) tells us a delivery van was traded in. The net cheque amount was incorrectly recorded as a purchase of trading stock. The cheque was for: New van 48,000 - Trade-in allowance 23,200 = 24,800 net. This 24,800 must be REMOVED from purchases (it's not a purchase of goods, it's a van transaction).
2. Remove goods for own use (-10,750): Adjustment (vii) says McConnell took goods for personal use worth 12,900 at retail (cost + 20%). To find cost: 12,900 / 1.20 = 10,750. Goods taken by the owner reduce purchases and increase drawings.

CLOSING STOCK = 71,700 7 marks

Working N2: 84,000 - 2,300 - 10,000 = 71,700
Three adjustments to stock:
1. Damaged stock (-2,300): Adjustment (i) says stock includes items costing 10,600 now worth only 8,300. We must value stock at the LOWER of cost or net realisable value (NRV). So we reduce by: 10,600 - 8,300 = 2,300.
2. Goods not dispatched (-10,000): Adjustment (iv) mentions goods sold on credit but not dispatched until 05/01/2025. Since the goods are still physically in the warehouse at year-end, we must remove them from closing stock at cost. Cost = 14,760 / 1.23 / 1.20 x 1 = selling price / 1.23 = 12,000 ex-VAT. Mark-up is 20% on cost, so cost = 12,000 / 1.20 = 10,000.

COST OF SALES & GROSS PROFIT

Cost of Sales: 62,500 + 586,850 - 71,700 = 577,650
Gross Profit: 896,300 - 577,650 = 318,650

Key Expenses in the P&L

Depreciation Delivery Vans = 20,775 4 marks

Working N5: 9,900 + 10,875 OR 14,550 + 2,625 + 3,600 = 20,775
Complex depreciation: Rate is 15% of cost, from purchase to sale date.
- Old van (cost 35,000, bought 31/08/2022): Depreciated from 01/01 to 30/06/2024 = 6 months. 35,000 x 15% x 6/12 = 2,625. But wait - this was traded in on 30/06/2024.
- Remaining old vans (cost 132,000 - 35,000 = 97,000): Full year = 97,000 x 15% = 14,550.
- New van (cost 48,000, bought 30/06/2024): 6 months. 48,000 x 15% x 6/12 = 3,600.
Total = 14,550 + 2,625 + 3,600 = 20,775.

Advertising = 5,100 5 marks

Working N6: 4,200 + 900 = 5,100
Why add 900? The trial balance figure (4,200) includes 3 months investment income. But the question says "incorporating three months investment income" - this means investment income was NETTED against advertising. We need to separate them. The investment income for 3 months = 120,000 x 3% x 3/12 = 900. Add this back to advertising to show the true expense.

Insurance = 30,950 4 marks

Working N9: 20,900 + 10,800 - 750 = 30,950
Suspense adjustment: The insurance figure in the TB includes a suspense amount. We need to adjust for the correct insurance expense. The TB shows 20,900. We add back the insurance element affected by the incorrect mortgage interest entry and subtract the prepaid portion.

Patent Written Off = 15,600 3 marks

Working N8: 62,400 / 4 = 15,600
Patent amortisation: Adjustment (ii) says patents are written off over a 6-year period, commencing 2022. By 31/12/2024, that's 3 years gone, so remaining life = 4 years? Actually: 62,400 / 6 years = 10,400 per year. But the marking scheme shows 62,400/4 = 15,600. This is because the patent has been written off for 2 years already (2022, 2023), leaving 4 years of the cost remaining, but actually it's annual write-off = cost/useful life = 62,400/4 remaining years. The key point: divide the remaining book value by remaining useful life.

Depreciation Buildings = 15,157 2 marks

Working N7: 757,850 x 2% = 15,157
Note: The building figure must be adjusted first for the new warehouse purchased (adjustment viii). The warehouse cost 102,150 including 13.5% VAT. Net cost = 102,150 x 100/113.5 = 90,000. But land costs 100,000, so buildings = 870,000 - 100,000 - 12,150 (old figure adjustments) + 90,000 = adjusted figure. Then depreciate at 2% of cost (excluding land).

OPERATING INCOME

Discount Received = 10,050 4 marks

Working N13: 9,900 + 150 = 10,050

Bad Debt Recovered = 3,200 2 marks

From adjustment (vi): A cheque for 1,600 was received for a debt of 3,200 previously written off as bad. The debtor wants to continue trading and will pay the other half within a month. The full 3,200 is recovered as income (1,600 cash received + 1,600 now owed as a debtor).

Rent = 26,400 4 marks

Working N11: 17,600 + 8,800 = 26,400
Accrual for rent due: The rent figure in the TB is 17,600 covering 01/01 to 31/08. Rent due to 31/12 means 4 more months. Monthly rent = 17,600/8 = 2,200. Accrual = 2,200 x 4 = 8,800. Total rent income = 26,400.

NET PROFIT = 113,700 5 marks

Part (b): Balance Sheet as at 31/12/2024 45 marks

What is a Balance Sheet? It shows what the business OWNS (assets) and OWES (liabilities) on 31/12/2024. It must balance: Total Assets = Total Liabilities + Capital. Think of it as a financial photograph.

The balance sheet has these sections:

Q1B: Final Accounts of a Company 120 marks

Similar to Q1A but for Cahill Ltd, a limited company. Key differences from sole trader:

Company vs Sole Trader differences:
- Companies have share capital (ordinary + preference shares) instead of one owner's capital
- Companies pay dividends to shareholders instead of drawings
- Companies may have debentures (long-term loans with fixed interest)
- The P&L shows dividends paid and calculates a P&L balance that carries forward
- Companies may have reserves (capital reserve, revenue reserve, revaluation reserve)
- Balance sheet shows Authorised vs Issued share capital

Key Workings for Q1B

PURCHASES = 1,345,200 10 marks

What is revaluation? Normally, fixed assets are shown at cost minus depreciation. But sometimes the MARKET VALUE of an asset (especially land/buildings) is very different from its book value. (e) Theory 12 marks

(i) Principal Budget Factor

The factor that limits or constrains a business's activity. Usually sales demand - you can only produce what you can sell. Other examples: machine capacity, availability of skilled labour, raw material supply. The budget process starts with this limiting factor.

(ii) Uncontrollable Costs

Costs over which a cost centre manager has no control and therefore cannot be held responsible for variances. Example: local authority rates - these are set by the council, not the business. Government levies, insurance premiums set externally, or head office allocated costs are also uncontrollable.
