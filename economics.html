Exam Paper Structure

The LC Economics Higher Level exam is 2.5 hours (9:30am-12:00pm) for 400 marks.

Top Exam Tips

1. Use economic terminology. "Opportunity cost" not "what you give up". "Demand-pull inflation" not "prices go up because people buy more".
2. Show workings in calculations. Formula first, then substitution, then answer. Partial credit for correct method even if answer is wrong.
3. Use data from charts/tables. When answering about trends, cite specific numbers: "CPI fell from 9.1% in January to 4.8% in June".
4. Justify opinion questions. When asked "Do you agree or disagree?", pick one side and give strong economic reasoning. No marks for fence-sitting.
5. Answer the question asked. "Outline one reason" = one reason only. Two reasons risks cancellation if one is wrong.
6. Know current Irish economic data. Budget 2025, minimum wage (€13.50), key policy measures, inflation trends.
7. Use descriptor marking to your advantage. In-depth knowledge + relates to question + concise + logical = full marks.

Key Economic Diagrams & Models

Essential diagrams you need to draw and label in the exam.

Supply and Demand

Equilibrium

Draw axes (Price vertical, Quantity horizontal). Supply curve slopes upward (S), Demand curve slopes downward (D). They intersect at equilibrium point E, with equilibrium price (Pe) and quantity (Qe).

Shifts: Increase in demand = D curve shifts RIGHT. Increase in supply = S curve shifts RIGHT.

Price Floor (Minimum Wage)

Labour market: Wage rate (vertical axis), Quantity of workers (horizontal). Supply of labour (SL) slopes up, Demand for labour (DL) slopes down.

Price floor (NMW): Horizontal line ABOVE equilibrium. At this wage: Qs > Qd = unemployment gap (excess supply of labour).

Long Run Average Cost (LRAC)

Costs (vertical), Quantity (horizontal). Draw multiple U-shaped SRAC curves. LRAC curve is the "envelope" touching the bottom of each SRAC.

Downward slope: Economies of scale (lower average costs as output increases).

Upward slope: Diseconomies of scale (higher average costs due to coordination problems, communication issues).

Monopoly Long Run Equilibrium

Price/Cost (vertical), Quantity (horizontal). Draw: Demand (D), Marginal Revenue (MR, below D), Marginal Cost (MC), Average Cost (AC).

Equilibrium: Where MC = MR. Draw vertical line up to Demand curve for price (P1), down to quantity (Q1). Mark AC at Q1. Profit = area between P1 and AC.

Key feature: Price > MC (allocative inefficiency). Supernormal profit in long run due to barriers to entry.

Oligopoly - Kinked Demand Curve

Price (vertical), Quantity (horizontal). Demand curve has a KINK at current price P1. Above P1 it's elastic (competitors don't follow price increase). Below P1 it's inelastic (competitors match price decrease).

MR curve: Has a vertical discontinuity (gap) at the kink point. This explains price rigidity in oligopolies.

Economic Cycle

Output/Real GDP (vertical), Time (horizontal). Wavy line showing peaks (boom) and troughs (recession).

Boom: Rising output, low unemployment, high consumer confidence, inflationary pressure.

Recession: Falling output, rising unemployment, low confidence, deflationary pressure.

Ireland 2008-2011: Deep recession due to banking crisis, property collapse, government debt crisis.

Key Formulas

Percentage Savings Rate
(Household Savings / Household Disposable Income) × 100
Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI)
Sum of squared market shares: (s₁)² + (s₂)² + (s₃)² + ...

HHI < 1500 = competitive | HHI > 2500 = highly concentrated

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)
PED = (% change in Qd) / (% change in P)

|PED| > 1 = elastic | |PED| < 1 = inelastic | |PED| = 1 = unit elastic

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)
YED = (% change in Qd) / (% change in Income)

YED > 1 = luxury | 0 < YED < 1 = necessity | YED < 0 = inferior good

Profit
Profit = Total Revenue - Total Cost
TR = Price × Quantity | TC = Fixed Cost + Variable Cost
Average Cost
AC = Total Cost / Quantity
Multiplier Effect
k = 1 / (1 - MPC) or k = 1 / MPS

Lower MPC (higher savings) = smaller multiplier = less economic stimulus

Economics Glossary

Search for any term. All definitions in plain language.

Cost Benefit Analysis
A method of weighing up whether the positive benefits of a project outweigh the associated costs, to decide whether it should proceed. For a project to go ahead, its benefits must equal or exceed its costs.
Opportunity Cost
The next best alternative forgone when making a choice. What you give up to get something else. E.g., if you spend €100 on concert tickets, the opportunity cost is what else you could have bought with that €100.
Scarcity
The fundamental economic problem: unlimited wants but limited resources. We cannot have everything we want, so we must make choices.
Positive Economic Statement
An objective, fact-based statement that describes the world as it is. It can be tested or verified using evidence/data. E.g., "Raising corporation tax by 3% would reduce FDI by 25%".
Normative Economic Statement
A subjective statement based on opinions or value judgments. It expresses what 'should' happen rather than what actually is happening. It cannot be tested using evidence. E.g., "All citizens deserve free healthcare".
Price Floor
A legal minimum price below which a good or service cannot be sold, set and enforced by the government. Purpose: protect incomes, stabilise markets. E.g., national minimum wage (€13.50/hour).
Price Ceiling
A legal maximum price above which a good or service cannot be sold. Purpose: make essential goods affordable. Can lead to shortages if set below equilibrium. E.g., rent controls.
Demand
The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at various prices over a given period. Law of demand: as price falls, quantity demanded rises (inverse relationship).
Supply
The quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to produce at various prices over a given period. Law of supply: as price rises, quantity supplied rises (direct relationship).
Equilibrium
The point where demand equals supply. At equilibrium price, quantity demanded = quantity supplied. No shortage or surplus. Market clears.
Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)
The responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price. Elastic (|PED| > 1) = large response. Inelastic (|PED| < 1) = small response. Determined by: substitutes available, necessity vs luxury, proportion of income, time period.
Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)
The responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in income. Normal goods (YED > 0): demand rises as income rises. Inferior goods (YED < 0): demand falls as income rises. Luxury (YED > 1), Necessity (0 < YED < 1).
Merit Good
A good that is under-consumed in a free market because individuals undervalue its benefits. Government intervenes to encourage consumption (subsidies, provision). E.g., education, healthcare, phone pouches in schools (reduce distraction, improve learning).
Public Good
A good that is non-rivalrous (one person's use doesn't reduce availability for others) and non-excludable (can't prevent people from using it). E.g., street lighting, national defence. Free market won't provide them (free rider problem).
Market Failure
When the free market fails to allocate resources efficiently. Causes: externalities, public goods, merit/demerit goods, imperfect information, monopoly power, inequality. Government intervention needed.
Externality
A cost or benefit experienced by a third party not directly involved in an economic transaction. Negative externality (pollution, noise) = over-production. Positive externality (education, vaccination) = under-production.
Greenwashing
A marketing practice where companies make false or exaggerated claims to appear more environmentally friendly than they actually are. Leads to market failure: imperfect information, consumer harm, genuine eco-products disadvantaged.
Environmental Sustainability
Meeting the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. Balancing economic growth with environmental protection and resource conservation.
Inflation
A sustained increase in the general price level over time. Purchasing power of money decreases. Measured by Consumer Price Index (CPI). Causes: demand-pull, cost-push.
Demand-Pull Inflation
Inflation caused by excess demand in the economy. "Too much money chasing too few goods". Occurs when aggregate demand exceeds aggregate supply. Often in a boom.
Cost-Push Inflation
Inflation caused by increases in production costs (wages, raw materials, energy). Producers pass higher costs on to consumers through higher prices. E.g., cocoa prices tripling → higher chocolate prices.
Disinflation
A reduction in the rate of inflation. Prices are still rising, but more slowly. E.g., CPI falling from 9.1% to 4.8% = disinflation (not deflation).
Deflation
A sustained decrease in the general price level. Negative inflation. Can be harmful: consumers delay purchases, businesses cut prices and wages, debt burden increases.
Consumer Price Index (CPI)
A measure of the average change in prices paid by consumers for a basket of goods and services over time. Used to measure inflation. Year-on-year % change in CPI = inflation rate.
Economic Boom
Phase of economic cycle with rising output, high employment, strong consumer/business confidence, rising wages and profits, inflationary pressure. Demand exceeds supply.
Economic Recession
Two consecutive quarters of negative economic growth (falling Real GDP). Rising unemployment, falling consumer spending and business investment, low confidence, deflationary pressure.
Unemployment
People of working age who are actively seeking work but cannot find employment. Types: cyclical (recession), structural (skills mismatch), frictional (between jobs), seasonal.
Full Employment
When everyone who wants a job at the current wage rate can find one (except frictional/seasonal unemployment). Ireland has maintained near-full employment since 2022. Positive: higher incomes, tax revenue. Negative: wage inflation, labour shortages.
Gig Economy
A labour market where short-term contracts or freelance work is widespread rather than permanent jobs. Workers have flexibility but less security. E.g., Deliveroo riders, Uber drivers. ~200,000 in Ireland.
Monopoly
A market with only ONE seller/producer. Characteristics: high barriers to entry, price maker, supernormal profit in long run, allocative inefficiency (P > MC). E.g., patent-protected pharmaceutical drug.
Oligopoly
A market dominated by a FEW large firms. Characteristics: interdependence, non-price competition, barriers to entry, kinked demand curve (price rigidity), collusion possible. E.g., mobile networks, supermarkets.
Perfect Competition
A theoretical market with many buyers and sellers, identical products, perfect information, no barriers to entry/exit. Firms are price takers, zero supernormal profit in long run. E.g., agricultural markets (close approximation).
Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI)
A measure of market concentration. Sum of squared market shares. HHI < 1500 = competitive. HHI 1500-2500 = moderate concentration. HHI > 2500 = highly concentrated. Used by competition authorities.
Barrier to Entry
Obstacles that prevent new firms from entering a market. Types: patents, high start-up costs, economies of scale, brand loyalty, control of resources, legal restrictions (licenses). Protect monopoly/oligopoly profits.
Economies of Scale
Average costs fall as output increases. Causes: technical (large machinery), managerial (specialisation), purchasing (bulk discounts), financial (cheaper credit), marketing (spread costs). Shown by downward-sloping LRAC.
Diseconomies of Scale
Average costs rise as output becomes too large. Causes: coordination problems, communication breakdown, low worker morale, managerial inefficiency. Shown by upward-sloping LRAC.
Fixed Cost
Costs that do not change with output in the short run. Must be paid even if output is zero. E.g., rent, insurance, salaries. Total cost = Fixed cost + Variable cost.
Variable Cost
Costs that change directly with output. Zero if output is zero. E.g., raw materials, hourly wages, electricity for production. Increase as more units produced.
Average Cost
Total cost per unit of output. AC = Total Cost / Quantity. U-shaped curve: initially falls (economies of scale), then rises (diseconomies).
Profit Maximisation
The objective of producing at the output level where the difference between total revenue and total cost is greatest. Occurs where MC = MR. But firms may have other objectives: sales maximisation, market share, satisficing, CSR.
Entrepreneurship
The willingness to take risks, organise factors of production, and innovate to create new businesses. Important for economic growth: creates jobs, drives innovation, increases competition, generates tax revenue.
Comparative Advantage
A country has comparative advantage in producing a good if it can produce it at a lower opportunity cost than other countries. Basis for specialisation and trade. Benefits: increased efficiency, higher output, lower prices, greater consumer choice.
Free Trade
Trade between countries without tariffs, quotas, or other restrictions. Benefits: lower prices, greater variety, economies of scale, competition, efficient resource allocation. Ireland benefits hugely as small, open economy.
Trade Protectionism
Government policies to restrict imports and protect domestic industries. Methods: tariffs, quotas, subsidies, standards, admin barriers. Reasons: protect jobs, infant industries, strategic sectors, reduce trade deficit. Costs: higher prices, retaliation, inefficiency.
Tariff
A tax on imported goods. Raises price of imports, making domestic products more competitive. Revenue for government. But: consumers pay more, trading partners may retaliate. E.g., EU tariffs on Chinese EVs.
Quota
A limit on the quantity of a good that can be imported. Directly restricts imports. Protects domestic producers but reduces consumer choice and raises prices.
Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)
Investment by a company or individual from one country into business interests in another country. Inward FDI (into Ireland): creates jobs, technology transfer, tax revenue. Outward FDI (from Ireland): diversification, profits from abroad.
Official Development Assistance (ODA)
Aid given by governments to support economic development and welfare in developing countries. Ireland's target: 0.70% of GNI (currently below target). Benefits LDCs: infrastructure, healthcare, education. Challenges: dependency, corruption.
World Trade Organisation (WTO)
International organisation promoting free trade. Functions: negotiate trade agreements, settle disputes, monitor trade policies, reduce barriers, provide technical assistance to developing countries.
Brexit
UK's exit from European Union (2020). Economic effects on Ireland: customs checks, tariffs, VAT on UK imports, sterling volatility, reduced UK car imports (more from Japan now).
Mixed Economy
An economic system combining elements of free market (private sector) and government intervention (public sector). Most modern economies including Ireland. Government provides public goods, regulates markets, redistributes income. E.g., private health insurance + public hospitals.
Government Debt
Total amount owed by government from accumulated budget deficits. Ireland measures as % of GNI* (more accurate than GDP due to multinational distortions). High debt = higher interest payments, less spending on services, tax increases.
Progressive Tax
A tax where the percentage rate increases as income increases. Higher earners pay a larger proportion of their income. E.g., income tax in Ireland (20% and 40% rates). Promotes equity.
Regressive Tax
A tax where lower earners pay a larger proportion of their income. E.g., VAT, excise duties. €1000 spent on VAT-able goods costs a low earner more as % of income than a high earner.
Value Added Tax (VAT)
Tax on consumption (spending). Charged at each stage of production/distribution but ultimately paid by final consumer. Ireland: standard rate 23%, reduced 13.5%/9%, zero 0%. Regressive but efficient to collect.
Multiplier Effect
An initial change in spending (e.g., government investment) leads to a larger final change in national income. k = 1/(1-MPC). If MPC = 0.8, multiplier = 5. €100m investment → €500m total increase in GDP. Leakages (savings, tax, imports) reduce multiplier.
Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC)
The proportion of extra income that is spent (consumed). If MPC = 0.75, for every €1 extra income, €0.75 is spent and €0.25 saved. Higher MPC = larger multiplier effect.
Marginal Propensity to Save (MPS)
The proportion of extra income that is saved. MPC + MPS = 1. Auto-enrolment pension scheme increases MPS (lower MPC), reducing multiplier in short term.
Public Infrastructure
Capital goods provided/funded by government for public use. E.g., roads, railways, water systems, schools, hospitals, broadband. Essential for economic growth. Insufficient investment → congestion, delays, reduced competitiveness.
Money Supply
Total amount of money in circulation in the economy. Controlled by Central Bank through interest rates, reserve requirements. Commercial banks create credit through lending (credit creation/money multiplier).
Credit Creation
Process by which commercial banks create money through lending. Factors affecting: reserve ratio, demand for loans, interest rates, central bank policy, economic confidence, regulation.
Residential Property Price Index (RPPI)
Measures changes in house prices in Ireland over time. Rising prices: wealth effect, but affordability crisis, labour market impacts (workers can't afford to live where jobs are), cost-of-living pressure.

Section B - Extended Response (300 marks)

Each question worth 75 marks. More detailed answers, calculations, diagrams required.

Calculations: Always show: 1) Formula, 2) Substitution with numbers, 3) Final answer with units. Even wrong answer gets marks for correct method.
Diagrams: Must be fully labelled. Axes, curves, equilibrium points, quantities, prices. Missing labels = lost marks.
Definitions: Use precise economic terminology. "Comparative advantage" not "being better at something".
Discussion questions: Structure: define term, give examples, analyse benefits/challenges, conclude. Each part builds on previous.
Agree/Disagree: State your position clearly in first sentence. Give 3-4 strong economic reasons. Don't waffle or sit on fence.

Golden Rules for Maximum Marks

1. Use economic terminology correctly. Learn the glossary. "Opportunity cost", "allocative efficiency", "market failure" - use the exact terms.
2. Always justify with economic reasoning. Don't just state facts. Explain WHY using economic principles. "This leads to X BECAUSE..."
3. Reference current Irish economic context. Budget 2025, minimum wage €13.50, inflation trends, government policies. Shows you're engaged.
4. Structure longer answers. Definition → Example → Analysis → Conclusion. Logical flow earns marks.
5. Answer all parts of multi-part questions. (a)(i)(ii)(iii) = three separate answers required. Missing one = lost marks.

Q1: Hosting Major Sporting Events 12.5 marks

Germany hosted Euro 2024 with 2.7 million attendees across 51 matches.

(a) Discuss arguments for and against 12

Q: Discuss one argument FOR and one AGAINST the view that hosting major sporting events benefits the host country's economy.

Argument FOR (6 marks)

Increased tourism revenue - The arrival of large numbers of international visitors boosts spending in local hotels, restaurants, tourist attractions. This creates a short-term injection into the circular flow of income.
Alternative answers: Job creation (temporary and permanent in construction, hospitality, event management) | Infrastructure development (improved transport with long-term benefits) | Boost to economic confidence (national pride, increased consumer spending).

Argument AGAINST (6 marks)

High cost - Expenses for infrastructure, security, event management may be substantial and surpass the benefits. The government must finance these through taxation or borrowing.
Alternative answers: Opportunity cost (funds can't be spent on healthcare, housing, education) | Risk of economic loss (if tourism revenue falls short of projections) | Underused facilities (specialised venues become white elephants after event) | Environmental damage (construction, pollution, pressure on resources).

(b) Cost benefit analysis definition 6

Q: Outline what you understand by the economic term cost benefit analysis.

Answer: A method that helps weigh up / evaluate whether the positive benefits outweigh the associated costs of a project, to decide whether or not it should proceed. For a project to go ahead, its benefits must equal or exceed its costs.
Key elements: Compare benefits vs costs | Used for decision-making | Applied to public sector projects (roads, hospitals) where profit motive doesn't apply.

Q2: Household Savings in Ireland 12.5 marks

Data for Q1 2024:

MeasureAmount
Household Disposable Income€43.91 billion
Household Consumption Expenditure€37.32 billion
Household Savings€6.59 billion

(a) Calculate savings rate 6

Q: Calculate the household percentage savings rate. Show all workings.

Formula: (Household Savings / Household Disposable Income) × 100 [1]
Substitution: (€6.59bn / €43.91bn) × 100 [1]
Answer: 15% [4]
Common error: Forgetting the % symbol loses 1 mark. Always include units!

(b) OR (c) - Internal choice

(b) Two economic factors affecting savings 6

Q: Outline two economic factors which households consider when deciding how much to save (other than their level of income).

1. Interest rates / access to credit [3] - Higher interest rates encourage savings as deposit returns increase. When borrowing is accessible, households save less and rely on credit. If borrowing is harder, households must save more.

2. Rate of inflation [3] - High/rising inflation encourages spending rather than saving (purchasing power declines). Low/stable inflation encourages saving (money retains value).
Other valid answers: Employment security (permanent job = save less, temporary = save more) | Economic outlook/consumer confidence | Government policies/taxation (DIRT, pension tax relief) | Household debt levels | Demographics/life stage | Availability of social protection | Security of savings (trust in banks).

(c) High savings - agree or disagree 6

Statement: A high level of household savings is desirable for the Irish economy.

AGREE arguments:
• Financial stability for households - cope better with emergencies, job losses, downturns
• Increased lending/investment by banks - more funds to lend to businesses and citizens
• Increased government investment - higher savings → banks invest in government bonds → lower borrowing costs
• Lower dependence on credit - reduces personal debt (lesson from 2008 crisis)
• Reduced pressure on social protection - less reliance on welfare, state pensions

DISAGREE arguments:
• Reduced consumer spending - weakens demand in retail, hospitality sectors
• Reduced economic growth / leakages - less spending → less revenue → job losses → slower growth
• Risk of deflation - less spending → businesses lower prices → reduced profits → less investment
• Lower government revenue - fall in spending → reduced VAT → less for healthcare, housing
• Increased income inequality - wealthier households save more, gap widens
Exam tip: Pick ONE side and defend it strongly with 3-4 reasons. Don't try to argue both sides or sit on the fence.

Q3: Used Car Imports - Japan vs UK 12.5 marks

Chart shows Irish imports of used cars from Japan increased dramatically from 2014 to 2023, while imports from UK decreased significantly.

(a) Economic reason for the shift 6

Q: Outline one economic reason why Irish consumers are switching to importing more used cars from Japan and fewer from the UK.

Brexit / Import costs - Since the UK left the EU, new trade barriers make it more expensive to import used cars from Britain. Irish buyers now face customs duties, VAT, and import fees which did not apply before Brexit. Extra costs make UK imports relatively less attractive compared to Japanese cars.
Alternative answers (all 6 marks):
• Sterling/euro exchange rate - Pound more unpredictable post-Brexit; when sterling appreciates, Irish pay more in euros
• Increased quality/reliability of Japanese cars - Better condition, lower mileage, superior technology impacts costs and resale value
• Japanese cars are cheaper - High turnover in Japan creates greater supply of second-hand cars at lower prices
• More EV/hybrid options - Japanese manufacturers (Toyota, Honda, Nissan) lead in hybrid/EV tech; cheaper to run with rising fuel costs
• Wider choice of models - Japan exports models never sold in UK/Ireland, giving buyers more variety
• Lower rust/corrosion - Japan uses less road salt, so cars are more durable long-term

(b) OR (c) - Internal choice

(b) Alternative to tariffs on Chinese EVs 6

Context: In 2024, the EU increased tariffs on EVs imported from China.

Q: Explain one alternative measure the EU could use (other than tariffs) to reduce imports of EVs from China.

Import quotas - The EU could set a cap on the number of EVs that can be imported from China every year. This would make it more difficult for Chinese EV manufacturers to expand their presence in the EU.
Alternative measures (all 6 marks):
• Subsidies for European EV producers - Financial support via grants, tax incentives for green tech makes EU EVs more competitive
• Stricter safety/environmental standards - Impose regulations Chinese EVs must meet; if they struggle to comply, imports reduce
• Administrative barriers/bureaucracy - Complex customs procedures, lengthy certification increase time and cost
• Changes to government procurement - Require public sector fleets to purchase only EU-manufactured EVs
• Investment restrictions - Limit Chinese investment in European car companies to prevent circumventing trade restrictions
• Investment in domestic battery production - Reduce cost advantage of Chinese EVs by producing batteries locally
• Trade embargo - Full ban on Chinese EVs (extreme measure, may violate WTO rules and trigger retaliation)

(c) Reason for rising protectionism 6

Statement: Trade protectionism seems to be increasing globally.

Q: Outline one reason why, in your opinion, trade protectionism seems to be increasing.

Political/strategic reasons / Deglobalisation - Sanctions and restrictions have occurred due to political decisions such as Brexit, responses to the war in Ukraine, recent trade policies by the United States. Governments face pressure from voters who feel globalisation has harmed local economies. Protectionist policies appeal to these voters by promising to support domestic industries.
Alternative reasons (all 6 marks):
• Reduce leakages from circular flow - Purchasing imports sends money abroad rather than keeping it in the home economy
• Protect domestic employment - Greater reliance on domestically produced goods keeps people in jobs
• Reduce pollution - Importing goods involves transportation which damages the environment; protectionism may reduce this
• Less reliance on foreign markets - Makes country more self-sufficient for essential goods/services
• Supply chain problems - COVID-19 highlighted weaknesses in global supply chains; countries prioritise domestic production of medical supplies, semiconductors
• National security concerns - Governments restrict trade in strategic industries (technology, energy) to safeguard sensitive sectors

Q4: Consumer Price Index for Chocolate 12.5 marks

Chart shows year-on-year % changes in Ireland's CPI for chocolate from January to June 2024.

(a) Comment on trend and benefit to consumers 6

Q: Comment on the trend in the CPI for chocolate shown, using data from the chart, and explain if the trend is beneficial for chocolate consumers.

Trend (using data)

The trend in the CPI for chocolate shows a steady decline from 9.1% in January 2024 to 4.8% in June 2024 (or by 4.3%). The trend represents a gradual slowdown in the rate of price increases. Chocolate prices are still rising, but at a decreasing rate (disinflation).

Beneficial or not?

BENEFICIAL: Slower price growth - prices increasing at lower rate makes chocolate less expensive than if inflation remained at 9.1% | Potential for price stability - may lead to stabilisation or reductions | Reduced financial pressure - easier to manage household budgets

NOT BENEFICIAL: Prices still increasing - disinflation only means rising more gradually, not falling; consumers still pay more than last year | Consumer budgets still affected - wage growth may not match chocolate price increases, so some must prioritise necessities over chocolate

(c) Demand-pull vs cost-push inflation 6

Context: In Q1 2024, cocoa prices tripled. Manufacturers expected to pass higher costs to shoppers in Q4 (Halloween/Christmas sales period).

Q: Do you regard this as demand-pull or cost-push inflation? Justify your choice.

Answer: Cost-push inflation
Justification:
• Rising input costs - Cocoa prices increased for chocolate producers; when raw material costs rise, businesses pay more to produce same goods (classic cost-push)
• Higher prices for consumers - Manufacturers faced with increased production costs pass these on by raising prices; inflation driven by higher costs
• Not caused by rise in demand - Demand-pull occurs when too much money chases too few goods / consumer demand rises faster than supply; here demand hasn't necessarily increased, but production costs have
• Supply-side shock - Sudden tripling of cocoa prices acts as supply shock, making chocolate production more expensive, reducing supply, contributing to rising prices
• Time lag in price increase - Producers expected to raise prices later in year; common with cost-push for businesses to absorb costs temporarily before passing on

Q5: Economic Statements 12.5 marks

(a) Define positive and normative statements 6

Positive Economic Statement (3 marks)

A positive economic statement is objective and fact-based. It describes the world as it is, or as it will be under certain conditions, without making value judgments. It can be tested/verified by using evidence/data.
Example: "If the Irish government were to raise corporation tax by 3%, it would reduce FDI by at least 25%." This can be tested with economic models and data.

Normative Economic Statement (3 marks)

A normative statement is subjective and based on opinions/value judgements. It expresses what 'should' happen rather than what actually is happening. It cannot be tested/verified by using evidence/data.
Example: "All citizens in Ireland deserve free healthcare to ensure equal access." This is an opinion about what should be, not a testable fact.

Q: Determine whether each statement below is positive or normative.

How to tell the difference:
Positive: Contains "if...then", makes predictions, can be tested, uses words like "is", "will", "does"
Normative: Contains "should", "ought", "deserve", "fair", "better" - value judgments that can't be proven true/false
