Top Exam Tips

1. Use the PCLM criteria. Every answer should include: Point, Context (quote/reference), Language (techniques), and your view/Meaning. This structure maximizes marks.
2. Quote precisely. Short, integrated quotes work best. "The writer describes the earth as a 'suspended jewel'" is better than copying whole paragraphs.
3. Name the techniques. Don't just say "effective language" - name the technique: metaphor, imagery, repetition, contrast, rhetorical question, emotive language, etc.
4. Plan your composition. Spend 10 minutes planning. A clear structure (intro, 3-4 developed points, conclusion) earns more than rambling.
5. Answer the question directly. If asked "To what extent do you agree?", state your position clearly and develop it. Don't just describe the text.
6. Write in paragraphs. One clear point per paragraph. Topic sentence, evidence, analysis, link to question.
7. Manage your time strictly. Don't spend 90 minutes on Section I comprehension and have only 30 minutes left for your composition. The composition is worth 100 marks.
8. Use literary terminology. First-person narrative, omniscient narrator, personification, anaphora, tone, diction, syntax. Shows sophistication.
9. In comparative, connect your texts. Use phrases like "Similarly...", "In contrast to...", "Both texts...", "While Text A shows... Text B reveals..."
10. Proofread. Save 5 minutes at the end. Check spelling, grammar, clarity. Cross out mistakes neatly rather than leaving them.

Writing Frameworks & Key Terms

Essential structures and techniques for compositions and literary analysis.

Essay Structure (Discursive/Personal)

Introduction
Hook → Context → Thesis Statement
Engage the reader, introduce the topic, state your position clearly.
Body Paragraphs (3-4)
Topic Sentence → Evidence/Example → Analysis → Link to Thesis
One clear idea per paragraph. Develop fully.
Conclusion
Restate Thesis → Summarize Main Points → Broader Implication/Final Thought
Don't introduce new ideas. Leave the reader thinking.

Short Story Structure

Opening
Set scene, introduce character, establish tone. Hook the reader immediately.
Rising Action
Build conflict, develop character, increase tension.
Climax
Turning point, moment of greatest tension or revelation.
Resolution
Resolve conflict, show character change, leave lasting impression.

Key techniques: Dialogue, descriptive language, show don't tell, varied sentence structure, sensory details.

Speech Structure

Opening
Greeting → Hook → Establish credibility → Preview main points
"Good morning. Imagine a world where..."
Body
3 main points, each developed with evidence, examples, anecdotes
Use rhetorical devices: repetition, rule of three, rhetorical questions
Conclusion
Summarize → Call to action → Memorable closing line
"So I ask you today..."

Techniques: Direct address ("you"), anaphora (repetition), emotive language, inclusive pronouns ("we"), contrast, humor.

Feature Article Structure

Headline
Catchy, informative title (often includes wordplay or intrigue)
Introduction
Engaging lead paragraph - anecdote, startling fact, or vivid description
Body
Develop topic with varied paragraphs: facts, quotes, examples, analysis
Use subheadings for structure
Conclusion
Circle back to opening, leave reader with insight or question

Tone: Engaging, accessible, informative. Mix facts with opinion/reflection.

PCLM Analysis Framework (For Comprehending Questions)

P - Point
State your point clearly and directly.
C - Context
Quote or reference the text specifically.
L - Language
Identify the technique used (metaphor, imagery, tone, etc.)
M - Meaning
Explain the effect/significance. Connect to the question.

Example: "Atwood uses humor to engage her audience (P). She states she's 'older than Donald Trump - and better looking!' (C). This self-deprecating humor and contrast (L) immediately establishes rapport with young listeners and shows she doesn't take herself too seriously (M)."

Literary Terms & Techniques Glossary

Search for any term. Essential vocabulary for analyzing texts and writing compositions.

Metaphor
A figure of speech that describes something by saying it IS something else. Creates direct comparison without 'like' or 'as'. Example: "The earth is a mother waiting for her children to return."
Simile
A comparison using 'like' or 'as'. Example: "Africa from space looks like a Turner painting."
Personification
Giving human qualities to non-human things. Example: "The way the planet seems to breathe, an animal unto itself."
Imagery
Vivid descriptive language that appeals to the senses (sight, sound, touch, taste, smell). Creates mental pictures for the reader.
Tone
The writer's attitude toward the subject or audience. Can be serious, humorous, sarcastic, optimistic, pessimistic, etc.
Diction
Word choice. Formal vs informal, technical vs accessible, emotive vs neutral. The specific words a writer selects.
Syntax
Sentence structure. How words and phrases are arranged. Can be simple, complex, fragmented, repetitive, varied.
Anaphora
Repetition of a word or phrase at the beginning of successive clauses or sentences. Example: "Once, there were no avocadoes... Once, children were taught to duck... Once, there was a world war..."
Rhetorical Question
A question asked for effect, not requiring an answer. Used to make the audience think or to emphasize a point.
Rule of Three
Listing three elements for emphasis and rhythm. Example: "inspiring visionaries, scientists, inventors, entrepreneurs, funders, policy makers, on-the-ground organisers."
Juxtaposition
Placing two contrasting ideas/images side by side for effect. Highlights differences.
Contrast
Emphasizing differences between two things, ideas, or time periods. Used to highlight change or opposing viewpoints.
Alliteration
Repetition of initial consonant sounds in neighboring words. Example: "plucky chancer."
Emotive Language
Words chosen to provoke an emotional response. Loaded with feeling or judgment.
Hyperbole
Deliberate exaggeration for emphasis or effect. Example: "I haven't yet killed off every single character in them."
Irony
Saying one thing but meaning another, or when the outcome is opposite to what's expected. Can be verbal, situational, or dramatic.
First-Person Narrative
Story told from the perspective of "I" or "we". The narrator is a character in the story.
Third-Person Narrative
Story told from an outside perspective using "he", "she", "they". Can be omniscient (all-knowing) or limited.
Omniscient Narrator
A narrator who knows everything about all characters and events. Can enter any character's mind.
Foreshadowing
Hints or clues about what will happen later in the narrative. Builds anticipation.
Flashback
Interruption of chronological narrative to show events from the past.
Symbolism
Using an object, character, or event to represent something deeper or more abstract. Example: a journey symbolizing personal growth.
Theme
The central idea or underlying message of a text. Universal concepts like love, power, justice, identity.
Motif
A recurring element (image, phrase, idea) that has symbolic significance and helps develop the theme.
Allegory
A narrative where characters and events represent abstract ideas or moral qualities. The story has both literal and symbolic meaning.
Allusion
A reference to another text, historical event, or cultural element. Assumes reader's knowledge.
Analogy
A comparison between two things to explain or clarify. Often used to make complex ideas accessible.
Apostrophe
Directly addressing an absent person, abstract concept, or inanimate object.
Bathos
An abrupt shift from the serious/elevated to the trivial/mundane, often for comic effect.
Dialogue
Conversation between characters. Reveals character, advances plot, provides information.
Euphemism
A mild or indirect word/phrase used instead of something harsh or blunt. Example: "passed away" for "died."
Pathos
Quality that evokes pity, sadness, or compassion in the audience/reader.
Ethos
Appeal to credibility or character. Establishing authority or trustworthiness.
Logos
Appeal to logic and reason. Using facts, statistics, logical arguments.
Oxymoron
Two contradictory words placed together. Example: "bittersweet", "deafening silence."
Paradox
A statement that appears self-contradictory but reveals a deeper truth.
Satire
Use of humor, irony, or exaggeration to criticize or expose flaws, often in society or politics.
Stream of Consciousness
Narrative technique that presents thoughts and feelings as they occur, without conventional structure.
Understatement
Deliberately representing something as less important or serious than it is, often for ironic effect.
Vernacular
Everyday language of a particular region or group. Informal, conversational speech.
Climax
The turning point or moment of greatest tension in a narrative. The peak of the action.
Denouement
The resolution or unraveling of the plot after the climax. Loose ends are tied up.
Protagonist
The main character or hero of a story. The character the reader typically follows.
Antagonist
The character or force that opposes the protagonist. Creates conflict.
Foil
A character who contrasts with another character (usually the protagonist) to highlight particular qualities.
Genre
A category of literature characterized by particular style, form, or content. Example: tragedy, comedy, romance, dystopia.
Internal Conflict
Psychological struggle within a character's mind. Character vs self.
External Conflict
Struggle between character and outside force. Character vs character, society, nature, or fate.

Mark Scheme Decoder

Understanding how English examiners award marks is crucial for maximizing your score.

Paper 1 - Section I: Comprehending (100 marks)

Question A Structure (50 marks) - Three parts: (i) 15 marks, (ii) 15 marks, (iii) 20 marks
Part (i) - Insights from text (15 marks) - Make THREE points. Each point with supporting reference = 5 marks. Must quote or reference the text specifically.
Part (ii) - Personal response (15 marks) - Develop TWO points minimum. Each point = ~7-8 marks. Must show clear personal engagement with the question.
Part (iii) - Language analysis (20 marks) - Discuss FOUR elements of style. Each element analyzed = 5 marks. Must NAME the technique and explain its EFFECT.

Paper 1 - Section I: Question B (50 marks)

Functional Writing Task - You're writing in a specific role (team captain, hotel manager, podcast contributor). Marks awarded for:
Content & Purpose (25 marks) - Addressing all bullet points, staying in role, achieving the purpose.
Coherence & Structure (15 marks) - Logical organization, paragraph structure, transitions.
Language & Style (10 marks) - Appropriate tone, vocabulary, sentence variety, technical accuracy.

Key point: You MUST address ALL the bullet points given in the question. Missing one = significant mark loss.

Paper 1 - Section II: Composing (100 marks)

Clarity of Purpose (P) - 30 marks - How well you understand and fulfill the task (essay, speech, story, etc.). Clear focus and sustained engagement.
Coherence of Delivery (C) - 30 marks - Structure, organization, logical flow. Introduction, development, conclusion. Paragraphing.
Efficiency of Language Use (L) - 30 marks - Vocabulary, sentence structure, style, tone appropriate to task. Sophistication and variety.
Accuracy of Mechanics (M) - 10 marks - Spelling, grammar, punctuation. Consistent tense. Technical correctness.

PCLM breakdown: Each category is marked independently. A piece with brilliant ideas but poor structure won't score full marks.

Paper 2 - Section I: Single Text (60 marks)

Relevance & Insight (15 marks) - Direct engagement with the question, depth of understanding, original thought.
Argument & Support (30 marks) - Quality of points, use of textual evidence (quotes/references), development of ideas.
Language & Style (10 marks) - Clarity, vocabulary, sentence structure, literary terminology.
Mechanics (5 marks) - Spelling, grammar, punctuation.

Note: Two questions per text - often one on power/themes, one on literary techniques & credibility.

Paper 2 - Section II: Comparative Study (70 marks)

Part (a) - 30 marks - Depth of engagement with ONE text. Clear understanding of the mode (Theme, Cultural Context, or General Vision).
Part (b) - 40 marks - Comparison of TWO other texts. Must explicitly COMPARE (similarities/differences), not just describe each separately.

Comparison markers award extra marks: "Similarly...", "In contrast...", "While Text A..., Text B...", "Both texts show..."

Mode-specific focus required: Theme/Issue questions focus on central ideas. Cultural Context questions focus on societal norms and values. General Vision questions focus on overall outlook (optimistic/pessimistic).

Paper 2 - Section III: Poetry (70 marks total)

A. Unseen Poem (20 marks) - Either Question 1 (two parts: mood description 10 marks + title evaluation 10 marks) OR Question 2 (single question on language use 20 marks).
B. Prescribed Poetry (50 marks) - Answer on ONE poet studied. Must discuss MULTIPLE poems. Marks for: relevance to question (10), quality of argument with textual support (30), language & style (7), mechanics (3).

Poetry technique terminology essential: Enjambment, caesura, assonance, consonance, meter, stanza structure, volta, etc.

Golden Rules for Maximum Marks

1. Use the PCLM structure. Point → Context (quote) → Language (technique name) → Meaning (effect/significance). This hits all marking criteria.
2. Quote intelligently. Short, integrated quotes work best. "The writer describes X as 'Y'" earns more marks than copying paragraphs without analysis.
3. Name the technique. Don't say "nice language" - say "metaphor", "emotive diction", "anaphora", "contrast". Shows literary knowledge.
4. Answer the question directly. If asked "To what extent...?", state your position clearly. Don't waffle or avoid committing.
5. Develop your points fully. One well-developed point with evidence > three superficial points without support.
6. Address ALL bullet points. In Question B (functional writing), every bullet point must be covered. Missing one loses major marks.
7. Compare explicitly in Comparative. Use comparison language. Don't write separate essays on each text.
8. Use literary terminology. Shows sophistication. First-person narrative, omniscient narrator, foreshadowing, juxtaposition, etc.
9. Plan before writing. 10 minutes planning your composition saves time and earns more marks. Structure matters.
10. Proofread. 5 minutes checking spelling, grammar, clarity. Cross out mistakes neatly. Mechanics marks matter.

Paper 1 - Text 1: The Underdog Effect 50 marks

Source: "The Underdog's Surprising Appeal" by David Robson (BBC Essential, 4 August 2024)

Theme: How the "underdog effect" influences our perspectives - why we support disadvantaged competitors.

Key Points from Text 1

Main Idea: We're psychologically drawn to support underdogs - those with odds stacked against them - even when it contradicts our desire for status and success.
Evidence Cited: Jamaican bobsled team (1988 Olympics), Donald Trump's 2016 campaign, Vandello's medal study (75% preferred country with fewer medals), sports team wealth experiment.
Key Finding: The underdog effect depends on perception of GENUINE disadvantage. If disadvantage comes from wealth or resources, sympathy disappears.
Psychological Explanation: We have heightened empathy for low-power individuals. We perceive high-power individuals as "colder". We assume underdogs work harder, making them more likeable.
Applications: Politics, justice decisions, conflict resolution, sports, personal inspiration ("anything is possible with hard work").

Question A - Part (i) 15 marks

Q: Based on your reading of TEXT 1, what insights do you gain about how the 'underdog effect' can influence our perspectives? Make three points, supporting your response with reference to the text.

Sample Answer Structure (3 points × 5 marks each):
Point 1: The underdog effect reveals that we often prefer emotional connection over logical self-interest when choosing who to support.
Context: The text notes that supporting underdogs "sits rather uncomfortably with humankind's known fixation on status and prestige" - logically we should align with likely winners "so that we can bask in their glory."
Insight: This shows our perspectives are shaped more by empathy and emotional response than rational calculation of benefits. [5 marks]
Point 2: Our perception of justice and fairness fundamentally shapes our loyalties and support.
Context: Vandello's research showed the underdog effect "shrank if they found that the team was awash with cash" - disadvantage must come from "genuine weakness" not lack of effort.
Insight: We don't support ALL disadvantaged parties equally - our perspective is influenced by whether we perceive the disadvantage as unjust or self-inflicted. [5 marks]
Point 3: The underdog effect shows we project our own aspirations onto others' struggles.
Context: The text explains we "often draw personal inspiration from these stories, which 'highlight that anything is possible with hard work and determination.'"
Insight: Our perspective is shaped by seeing ourselves in the underdog's position - their potential success represents hope for our own challenges. [5 marks]

Question A - Part (ii) 15 marks

Q: To what extent do you agree with the writer's claim, in TEXT 1, that humankind has a "fixation on status and prestige"? Develop at least two points in your response.

Sample Answer Structure (2+ points, ~7-8 marks each):
Approach: State your position clearly (agree/disagree/qualified agreement), then develop with personal reflection, examples, counter-arguments.
Point 1 - Agreement: Social media demonstrates our obsession with status. Platforms like Instagram and TikTok are built entirely around follower counts, likes, and visible markers of success. People curate perfect lives to project prestige. This supports Robson's claim - we ARE fixated on status.
[7-8 marks for development with specific examples]
Point 2 - Counter-argument: However, the very existence of the underdog effect contradicts this fixation. If we were truly obsessed with status, we'd only support winners. The fact that 75% of study participants preferred countries with FEWER medals shows something more complex than simple prestige-seeking. Perhaps we're conflicted - wanting status but also valuing authenticity, effort, and justice.
[7-8 marks for developed counter-argument]

Question A - Part (iii) 20 marks

Q: "In TEXT 1, David Robson uses elements of language that are effective in making this article both informative and thought-provoking." To what extent do you agree with this statement? Support your answer with reference to four elements of Robson's style from the text.

Sample Answer Structure (4 elements × 5 marks each):
Element 1 - Concrete Examples: Robson grounds abstract psychological concepts in recognizable examples - "From fairy tale's Cinderella and fictional boxer Rocky, to real-world activist Erin Brockovich." This makes complex ideas accessible and informative by connecting them to cultural touchstones readers know. The mix of fiction and reality is thought-provoking as it shows the effect spans both worlds. [5 marks]
Element 2 - Scientific Evidence: Robson cites specific research with precise data: "75% preferred the team with the fewer medals." This statistical specificity makes the article informative and credible. The unexpected nature of the finding (three-quarters!) is thought-provoking - it challenges assumptions about human behavior. [5 marks]
Element 3 - Rhetorical Questions: He poses questions readers likely ask themselves: "Why would we willingly put ourselves through disappointment by supporting the 'back runner'?" This technique is thought-provoking as it engages readers directly, while the subsequent answer is informative. The question-answer structure creates a conversational, accessible tone. [5 marks]
Element 4 - Contrasting Ideas: Robson juxtapositions expectations against reality - the underdog effect "sits rather uncomfortably with humankind's known fixation on status and prestige." This contrast is thought-provoking because it highlights paradoxes in human nature, while informatively explaining the tension between competing psychological drives. [5 marks]

Question B 50 marks

Task: You are the captain of a school's sports team, about to compete as the underdogs in the final of a national competition. Write the text of the pre-match team-talk you would deliver.

Must include:

  • Outline aspects of the game plan you'll employ on the field
  • Remind them of the strengths and weaknesses of your opponents
  • Motivate them to overcome the odds and achieve victory
Sample Opening:
"Listen up, team. Gather round. I know what everyone's been saying all week - we're the underdogs. We shouldn't even be here. They've got the bigger budget, the fancier facilities, the star players everyone's heard of. But you know what? That's exactly why we're going to win today..."
Structure Tips:
1. Acknowledge underdog status - Show you understand the challenge but reframe it as an advantage (no pressure, element of surprise, motivation).
2. Game plan specifics - Describe actual tactics: "We'll press high in the first 20 minutes while they're overconfident... focus on quick transitions... exploit their weak left side..."
3. Opponent analysis - "Their striker is fast but predictable - always cuts inside. Their defense is solid but slow to turn. They expect us to sit back - we won't."
4. Emotional appeal - Reference shared struggles, past victories, belief in each other. Use "we" language. Build to crescendo.
5. Call to action - End with specific, actionable instruction and rallying cry: "So when that whistle blows, we hit them with everything we've got. Are you ready?"

Paper 1 - Text 2: Margaret Atwood Speech 50 marks

Source: Speech at One Young World Congress, Montreal, September 2024

Theme: A "wise old counsellor" offers advice and perspective on world problems to young people.

Key Points from Text 2

Tone: Humorous, self-deprecating, yet ultimately serious and inspiring. Atwood uses comedy to engage before delivering weighty messages.
Opening Strategy: Jokes about age, dystopian novels, naps after lunch. Establishes rapport and shows she doesn't take herself too seriously.
Historical Perspective: "Cheer up, it's been worse." References French Revolution, World War II, bubonic plague, atomic bomb drills. Message: humanity has survived catastrophes before.
Current Challenges: Climate change as the "worst one now" - brings wildfires, floods, droughts, migration. Also mentions old problems: wars, economic inequality, authoritarian regimes.
Solutions Focus: Highlights innovations already underway: marine parks, mushrooms that eat plastic, hydrogen fuels, new foods. Emphasizes THREE requirements: lower carbon, affordability, attractiveness.
Call to Action: "All beneficial actions must be local and grounded" BUT must also scale internationally. Requires collaboration: "indigenous peoples, scientists, inventors, entrepreneurs, funders, policy makers."
Closing: "Go. For. It." - Direct, emphatic, optimistic command.

Question A - Part (i) 15 marks

Q: Based on your reading of TEXT 2 what insights do you gain about Margaret Atwood's perspective on current world problems? Make three points, supporting your response with reference to the text.

Sample Answer Structure:
Point 1: Atwood believes current crises should be contextualized within human history rather than viewed in isolation.
Context: She emphasizes "Cheer up, it's been worse" and lists historical catastrophes - world wars that "killed an estimated 80 million people" and plagues that "killed half the population of Europe."
Insight: Her perspective is that while problems are serious, humanity has proven resilient. Historical context prevents despair. [5 marks]
Point 2: She views climate change as fundamentally different from past problems because it creates cascading, interconnected challenges.
Context: She calls climate change "the worst one now" and shows how it brings "whirlwind wildfires, floods, soil erosion, violent winds, droughts, bad harvests" and "human migration on a vast scale."
Insight: Unlike isolated historical catastrophes, this crisis is systemic - one problem triggers multiple others. Her perspective emphasizes interconnectedness. [5 marks]
Point 3: Atwood believes solutions exist but require both innovation AND pragmatism to succeed.
Context: She lists innovations but stresses they must be "cheap enough so that many, many people will be able to deploy them" and "attractive enough so people will want to actually use them."
Insight: Her perspective is realistic - she knows brilliant ideas fail if they're unaffordable or unappealing. Real-world implementation matters more than theoretical perfection. [5 marks]

Question A - Part (iii) 20 marks

Q: "In TEXT 2, Margaret Atwood uses elements of language that are effective in making this speech both engaging and inspiring." To what extent do you agree with this statement? Support your answer with reference to four elements of Atwood's style from the text.

Sample Answer Structure:
Element 1 - Self-Deprecating Humor: Atwood immediately engages the audience with jokes: "I'm older than Donald Trump - and better looking!" and "One day you will have to take naps after lunch. So, cherish the moment." This humor is engaging because it's unexpected from a literary icon, and inspiring because it shows humility - she positions herself as approachable rather than superior. [5 marks]
Element 2 - Anaphora (Repetition): She uses powerful repetition with "Once..." to list historical hardships: "Once, there were no avocadoes... Once, children were taught to duck... Once, there was a world war..." This repetition is engaging rhythmically - it builds momentum like a drumbeat - and inspiring because the implicit message is "those times passed, these will too." [5 marks]
Element 3 - Concrete Examples: Rather than abstract discussion, Atwood lists specific innovations: "mushrooms that eat plastics", "organic fibres made from sea algae, bamboo, and bananas", "chia seeds in 1955." These concrete details are engaging because they're surprising and memorable, and inspiring because they prove solutions are REAL and happening now, not theoretical. [5 marks]
Element 4 - Direct Address: She speaks directly to the audience: "You yourselves will know", "you've got the energy and optimism", "I'm very much looking forward to seeing what you'll come up with." This second-person address is engaging because it creates intimacy and conversation rather than lecture, and inspiring because it explicitly expresses belief in their capabilities. [5 marks]

Question B 50 marks

Task: You are a hotel manager. A recent, disgruntled guest has left a highly critical review on a travel-review website. Write your online response.

Must include:

  • Outline the proud tradition and history of your establishment
  • Challenge the specific criticisms of the guest reviewer
  • Encourage the reviewer to return by highlighting upcoming events in your hotel and its locality
Sample Structure:
Opening: Thank them for feedback (professional tone), acknowledge their disappointment, introduce hotel history naturally.
History: "The Grand Harbour Hotel has welcomed guests since 1887, earning our reputation through..." - brief but proud, not defensive.
Address Criticisms: Be specific. If they complained about noise: "I was surprised to read about noise disturbance as our rooms were renovated in 2023 with soundproofing..." Challenge factually, not emotionally.
Invite Return: "I would love the opportunity to restore your faith in our service. This summer we're hosting..." - list specific events, special offers, local attractions.
Tone: Professional, courteous, confident but not arrogant. Acknowledge without groveling. Focus on solutions.

Paper 1 - Text 3: Planet Earth from Space 50 marks

Source: Extracts from Samantha Harvey's novel "Orbital" (2024)

Theme: Six astronauts in a spacecraft observe Earth from space - perspective shift from viewing the planet from outside.

Key Points from Text 3

Setting: H-shaped spacecraft, six astronauts (American, Japanese, British, Italian, two Russian), orbiting Earth, nine-month mission.
Initial Attraction: Night views - "gorgeous encrusting of city lights", "thick embroidered urban tapestries", electric excess. Can point to home cities.
Perspective Shift: "After a week or so" they come to love the daytime earth - "humanless simplicity of land and sea", "the way the planet seems to breathe, an animal unto itself."
Key Imagery: Earth as mother ("Mother Earth... waiting for her children to return"), as lover ("exulted lover"), as miracle ("suspended jewel so bright").
Unity Message: At night, only ONE man-made border visible (Pakistan-India). "By day even that has gone." Shows Earth's natural unity despite human divisions.
Emotional Response: "A desire takes hold... the need (fuelled by fervour) - to protect this huge yet tiny earth." Seeing Earth's fragility inspires protective instinct.
Future Perspective: Roman contemplates space station ending through "restless spirit of endeavour" - "Striking out, further and deeper. The moon, Mars." Human nature: "not made to stand still."

Question B 50 marks

Task: You are a contributor to a podcast entitled "Eyes Wide Open" where you reflect on how your perspective on a significant issue changed as a result of an experience or encounter.

Must include:

  • Identify the issue and explain your previous attitude towards it
  • Describe the experience or encounter that changed your perspective
  • Consider some of the life lessons that you and others can learn from this reflection
Sample Structure:
Opening: "Welcome back to Eyes Wide Open. I'm [name], and today I want to talk about the moment I realized..." - podcast tone, conversational.
The Issue: Choose something meaningful - climate change, homelessness, mental health, immigration, technology addiction. State your previous view clearly: "I used to think... I believed... My attitude was..."
The Experience: Tell the story vividly. Use sensory details. Build to the moment of realization. "That's when it hit me..." Make it personal and specific.
Life Lessons: Extract broader wisdom: "What I learned is...", "The lesson here is...", "If there's one thing listeners can take away..." Connect personal experience to universal truths.
Closing: "So that's my story. Thanks for listening to Eyes Wide Open. I'd love to hear about your perspective-changing moments..." - podcast sign-off.

Paper 1 - Section II: Composing Options 100 marks

Choose ONE composition from seven options. All compositions are marked using PCLM criteria (Purpose 30, Coherence 30, Language 30, Mechanics 10).

The Seven Options (2025 Paper)

1. Discursive Essay: "Consider the power and value of both printed and digital photographic images today."
Approach: Explore both types. Printed photos = tangible, permanent, curated. Digital = instant, shareable, abundant. Argue which has more value or how they serve different purposes.
2. Speech: For/against the motion: "Truth has become a valueless currency in today's world."
Approach: Strong opening, clear position, 3-4 developed arguments, rhetorical devices (repetition, rhetorical questions, anecdotes), call to action. Address audience directly.
3. Short Story: "A 'plucky chancer' challenges a more privileged or established opponent."
Approach: Create vivid characters, build tension, show the challenge through action/dialogue, resolve with impact. Clear underdog narrative. Show don't tell.
4. Personal Essay: "Reflect on some of the disappointments you have experienced and the impact they have had on you."
Approach: Honest, reflective tone. Choose 2-3 specific disappointments. Show growth/learning. Balance vulnerability with insight. Avoid self-pity - focus on understanding.
5. Feature Article: "'A love letter to Ireland' - explore reasons to view contemporary Irish society through an optimistic lens."
Approach: Catchy headline, engaging opening, mix of facts and opinion, varied paragraphs, conversational but informative tone. Celebrate specific aspects of Irish life/culture/progress.
6. Personal Essay: "Reflect on factors that would influence your voting intentions in future elections."
Approach: Identify 3-4 key factors (policies, character, vision, track record). Explain WHY each matters to you. Show political awareness without being partisan. Balance idealism and pragmatism.
7. Short Story: "An ambitious character whose reckless actions lead to disaster."
Approach: Create compelling protagonist, establish their ambition clearly, show escalation of recklessness, build to catastrophic climax. Tragedy structure. Internal and external conflict.

Choosing Your Composition

Play to your strengths. If you're good at storytelling, choose a short story. If you prefer argument, choose speech or discursive essay. If you like personal reflection, choose personal essay.
Consider the time. Short stories need careful pacing and editing. Essays can be more straightforward. Speeches need rhetorical flair but clear structure.
Check you can develop 3-4 substantial points. Before committing, mentally outline your main ideas. If you can only think of 1-2 points, choose a different topic.
Think about language opportunities. Which task lets you show vocabulary range, varied sentences, sophisticated style? That's how you maximize the Language marks (30).

Question Types (Pattern across all texts)

Type 1: Power/Justice/Themes Question

Example: "Writers employ a range of literary techniques to heighten the credibility of characters. Choose TWO characters and discuss effectiveness of techniques used."
Approach: Choose characters carefully (complex ones give more to discuss). Identify specific techniques: dialogue, internal monologue, description, actions, relationships, development. Explain how each technique makes the character BELIEVABLE/AUTHENTIC.

Sample Question - The Crucible

Option (ii): "Miller's exploration of the conflict between obeying one's conscience or betraying one's conscience reveals a variety of compelling insights into human nature." Discuss.

Sample Structure:
Introduction: State your position - agree, show you understand the question. Preview the insights you'll discuss.
Point 1: John Proctor's arc shows conscience vs survival. His confession recantation - "I have given you my soul; leave me my name!" Shows human need for integrity even at ultimate cost. Reveals we define ourselves by moral choices.
Point 2: Reverend Hale's journey shows conscience evolution. Initially obeys authority blindly, later tries to save lives even if it means encouraging lies. Reveals conscience isn't fixed - it develops through experience and moral awakening.
Point 3: Abigail represents conscience suppression. She betrays hers repeatedly but shows no remorse - self-preservation dominates. Reveals how power and self-interest can silence moral voice entirely.
Point 4: Rebecca Nurse embodies unwavering conscience. Refuses to confess to lies even facing death. Her steadfastness reveals the strength some find in moral certainty and faith.
Conclusion: Miller shows conscience as battlefield - between self and society, survival and integrity, expediency and principle. Compelling because every character faces this conflict differently.

Paper 2 - Section II: Comparative Study 70 marks

Answer ONE question from either: A - Theme or Issue, B - Cultural Context, or C - General Vision and Viewpoint.

IMPORTANT: Cannot use the text from Section I. Must use texts prescribed for comparative study. Maximum ONE film.

The Three Modes

THEME OR ISSUE: Focus on central ideas explored across texts. Common themes: power, justice, identity, relationships, ambition, isolation, coming of age, conflict, survival, love, betrayal, truth, freedom.
CULTURAL CONTEXT: Focus on the society/world of the texts - values, norms, expectations, social structures, attitudes, historical period. How does the cultural context shape characters and events?
GENERAL VISION AND VIEWPOINT: Focus on the overall outlook/tone - optimistic vs pessimistic, hopeful vs despairing, light vs dark. Is the world presented as fundamentally good or bad? Are characters able to overcome challenges or crushed by them?

Sample Question - Cultural Context

2(a): In ONE text, discuss the extent to which understanding the consequences of not conforming to societal norms influences the behaviour of a central character (30 marks).

2(b): In TWO other texts, compare the extent to which this understanding influences behaviour of a central character in each (40 marks).

Sample Structure:
Part (a) - 30 marks (ONE TEXT):
Choose a text where a character faces pressure to conform. Example: In "The Crucible", John Proctor understands that refusing to confess means death, but his internal conflict shows how awareness of consequences (execution vs life) ultimately strengthens rather than weakens his resistance. His behavior shifts from private guilt to public defiance BECAUSE he understands what's at stake - his understanding makes him choose conscience over survival. Develop with quotes, scenes, analysis.
Part (b) - 40 marks (TWO OTHER TEXTS - MUST COMPARE):
Text 2 example: In "Brave New World", Bernard understands consequences of nonconformity (exile to Iceland) which makes him CAREFUL - he criticizes privately but conforms publicly until forced into open rebellion. CONTRAST with...
Text 3 example: In "The Great Gatsby", Gatsby completely IGNORES consequences of not conforming to class boundaries - his understanding doesn't influence his behavior because his obsession overrides caution.
KEY: Use comparison language - "Similarly...", "In contrast...", "While Bernard..., Gatsby...", "Both texts show..."

Comparison Tips

1. Don't write two separate essays. Weave texts together. Compare point by point, not text by text.
2. Use comparison markers. Similarly, in contrast, both, whereas, while, however, likewise, unlike, just as.
3. Find meaningful connections. Look for similarities AND differences. The most interesting comparisons show nuance.
4. Stay focused on the mode. Theme questions need theme focus. Cultural Context questions need society focus. Don't drift between modes.
5. Quote from all texts. Show detailed knowledge of each. Short, integrated quotes work best.

Approach for Unseen Poetry:
1. Read carefully 2-3 times. Understand literal meaning first, then deeper themes.
2. Identify techniques. Look for imagery, metaphor, alliteration, enjambment, caesura, rhyme scheme, repetition, wordplay.
3. Consider structure. How do stanzas work? Where's the volta (turn)? How does it open vs close?

Part B: Prescribed Poetry (50 marks)

Answer on ONE poet you studied. Must discuss MULTIPLE poems.

The Poets (2025)

  • Eavan Boland - "evocative imagery... explores complex nature of powerlessness"
  • Patrick Kavanagh - "simple language... powerful insights into nature and human nature, from parochial perspective"
  • T.S. Eliot - "clever use of imagery... vivid portrayal of morally bankrupt and futile world"
  • Derek Mahon - "appreciation enhanced if poetry read aloud... language and thematic concerns"
  • Tracy K. Smith - "effective use of tone... heighten thought-provoking questions about nature of our lives"
Sample Answer Structure (Eavan Boland example):
Introduction: State your agreement with the statement. Preview which poems you'll discuss and the main argument. "Boland's imagery is indeed evocative and essential to her exploration of powerlessness, particularly in poems like 'Child of Our Time', 'The Famine Road', and 'This Moment'..."
Paragraph 1: "Child of Our Time" - Analyze specific images (bomb, ordinary street becomes warzone). Show how imagery makes abstract powerlessness (civilian casualties) visceral and emotional. Quote: "Your death was not an incident in a war, / it was a crime." The stark imagery of the child's death evokes the ultimate powerlessness - innocent life ended by forces beyond control.
Paragraph 2: "The Famine Road" - Imagery of physical labor, broken bodies, road-building as futile. "this had been hunger" - the past tense imagery evokes historical powerlessness. The road itself becomes symbol of oppression - forced labor that benefits others.
Paragraph 3: "This Moment" - Domestic imagery (woman, child, doorway, summer dusk). Evokes different type of powerlessness - trapped in domestic sphere, waiting. The beauty of the imagery (moths, light, tenderness) contrasts with the stasis, making the powerlessness poignant rather than angry.
Conclusion: Synthesize - Boland's imagery works across different contexts (political violence, historical oppression, domestic confinement) to make powerlessness tangible, emotional, and complex. The evocative nature of her images ensures we FEEL the powerlessness, not just understand it intellectually.

Poetry Analysis Tips

1. Name specific poems. Don't write vaguely about "Boland's poetry" - cite actual titles.
2. Quote precisely. Short, relevant quotes integrated into sentences. Show you know the poems intimately.
3. Use technical terminology. Enjambment, caesura, volta, imagery, metaphor, assonance, alliteration, meter, stanza structure, tone, diction.
4. Discuss multiple poems. Aim for 3-4 poems discussed in depth rather than 6-7 mentioned briefly.
5. Stay focused on the question. If it asks about imagery, focus on imagery. If it asks about tone, focus on tone. Don't write a generic essay.
6. Show relationships between poems. How do different poems explore the same theme differently? Find patterns and contrasts within the poet's work.
