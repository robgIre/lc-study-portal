Exam Paper Structure

The LC Chemistry Higher Level exam is 3 hours (2:00-5:00pm) for 400 marks. You answer any 8 questions from 11 available. All questions carry 50 marks each.

SectionQuestionsTopicsNotes

Section BQ4 - Q11Theory, Calculations, Organic, AppliedQ4 is always short questions. Q5-11 are longer topic-based questions.

Key point: There are NO restrictions on which questions to answer. Pick your best 8 from the 11 available.

Top Exam Tips

1. Always state units! One mark is deducted for missing or incorrect units. Write cm³, mol L⁻¹, kJ mol⁻¹ etc.
2. Show ALL workings. Each step of a calculation earns marks separately. A wrong final answer with correct method still earns most marks.
3. Learn the mandatory experiments. Section A questions are based on experiments you did in class. They appear EVERY year and are very predictable.
4. Use key words from the marking scheme. In theory questions, specific words earn marks. "Proton donor" not just "gives away hydrogen".
5. Draw labelled diagrams. Diagrams earn marks and often explain better than words. Label everything.
6. Q4 is guaranteed marks. Short questions covering many topics. Answer your best 8 from the options given. Each part is only 6 marks.
7. Use the log tables! The Formulae and Tables booklet is provided. Use it for atomic masses, constants, and formulas.

Key formulas organised by topic. The Formulae and Tables booklet is provided in the exam - these are the ones you need to know how to USE.

Moles & Concentration

Number of Moles
n = mass / Molar mass (Mr)
Concentration (Molarity)
c = n / V (in litres)

To convert cm³ to litres: divide by 1000

Moles from Solution
n = c × V / 1000 (when V is in cm³)
Avogadro's Law
At STP: 1 mole of gas = 22.4 L | At room temp: 1 mole = 24.0 L
Avogadro's Constant
NA = 6.0 × 10²³ mol⁻¹

Number of particles = n × NA

Acids & Bases

pH
pH = -log₁₀[H₃O⁺]
H₃O⁺ from pH
[H₃O⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ
Weak Acid pH
[H⁺] = √(Ka × Macid)

Then pH = -log₁₀[H⁺]

Ionic Product of Water
Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻]

At 25°C: Kw = 1.0 × 10⁻¹⁴ mol² L⁻²

pH of Pure Water
[H⁺] = √Kw

At 25°C: pH = 7.0 | At 60°C (Kw = 9.61 × 10⁻¹⁴): pH = 6.5

Thermochemistry

Heat Energy
q = mcΔT

m = mass (kg), c = specific heat capacity (kJ kg⁻¹ K⁻¹), ΔT = temperature change (K)

Heat of Reaction
ΔH = q / n (kJ mol⁻¹)

Exothermic: ΔH is negative (heat released) | Endothermic: ΔH is positive (heat absorbed)

Hess's Law
ΔHreaction = Σ ΔHf(products) - Σ ΔHf(reactants)

OR use: reverse signs when reversing equations, multiply when scaling

Equilibrium

Equilibrium Constant (Kc)
For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD: Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ

Concentrations in mol L⁻¹ (divide moles by volume in litres)

Rates of Reaction

Average Rate
Rate = Change in volume (or mass) / Change in time
Instantaneous Rate
Draw a tangent to the curve at the required time. Rate = slope of tangent.

Atomic Structure

Relative Atomic Mass
Ar = Σ(isotope mass × % abundance) / 100
Mass Number
A = protons + neutrons
Electronegativity Difference & Bond Type
< 0.4 = non-polar covalent | 0.4-1.7 = polar covalent | > 1.7 = ionic

Dilution & Concentration

Dilution
c₁V₁ = c₂V₂
% (w/v) Solution
Mass of solute (g) / Volume of solution (cm³) × 100
ppm (parts per million)
mg per litre (mg L⁻¹)

Empirical Formula

Steps
1. Divide each % by atomic mass → ratio 2. Divide all by smallest → whole number ratio 3. That gives the empirical formula

Chemistry Glossary

Search for any term. All definitions written in plain language.

Atom
The smallest particle of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element. Made up of protons, neutrons (in the nucleus) and electrons (orbiting the nucleus).
Isotope
Atoms of the same element that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. They have the same atomic number but different mass numbers. Example: bromine-79 and bromine-81.
Relative Atomic Mass
The average mass of an atom of an element compared to one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 atom. Takes into account the masses and abundances of all isotopes.
Mass Spectrometer
An analytical instrument used to determine the relative abundance of isotopes of a given mass number. Separates ions based on their mass-to-charge ratio.
Atomic Orbital
A region in space around the nucleus where there is a high probability of finding an electron. Types: s (spherical), p (dumbbell), d (complex shapes).
Electron Configuration
The arrangement of electrons in an atom's orbitals. Written as 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ etc. Electrons fill lowest energy orbitals first (Aufbau principle).
Atomic (Covalent) Radius
Half the distance between the nuclei of two atoms of the same element joined by a single covalent bond. Increases down a group, decreases across a period.
Electronegativity
The relative attraction that an atom in a molecule has for the shared pair of electrons in a covalent bond. Fluorine is the most electronegative element (3.98).
Ionic Bond
A bond formed by the transfer of electrons from one atom to another, creating oppositely charged ions that attract each other. Forms between metals and non-metals (electronegativity difference > 1.7).
Covalent Bond
A bond formed by the sharing of a pair of electrons between two atoms. Non-polar if atoms have similar electronegativity; polar if they differ significantly.
Polar Molecule
A molecule where the centres of positive and negative charge do not coincide, creating a permanent dipole. Requires polar bonds AND an asymmetric shape (e.g., NF₃ is polar, BF₃ is not).
Van der Waals Forces
Weak intermolecular forces caused by temporary dipoles in molecules. Present in ALL molecules. Strength increases with molecular size.
Dipole-Dipole Forces
Intermolecular forces between polar molecules. The positive end of one molecule attracts the negative end of another. Stronger than Van der Waals forces.
Hydrogen Bonding
A strong intermolecular force between a hydrogen atom bonded to N, O, or F and a lone pair on another N, O, or F atom. Explains water's high boiling point.
Brønsted-Lowry Acid
A proton (H⁺) donor. A strong acid is a good proton donor (fully dissociates). A weak acid is a poor proton donor (partially dissociates).
Brønsted-Lowry Base
A proton (H⁺) acceptor. Bases accept protons from acids in acid-base reactions.
Conjugate Acid-Base Pair
Two species that differ by a single proton (H⁺). Example: HCO₃⁻ and H₂CO₃ are a conjugate pair (they differ by one H⁺).
pH
A measure of the acidity of a solution. pH = -log₁₀[H₃O⁺]. pH 7 = neutral, pH < 7 = acidic, pH > 7 = basic. Each pH unit represents a 10-fold change in H⁺ concentration.
Indicator
A weak acid that changes colour depending on pH. Different indicators change at different pH ranges. Must be chosen to match the pH at the equivalence point of a titration.
Catalyst
A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction by providing an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy. It is not consumed in the reaction.
Heterogeneous Catalysis
Catalysis where the catalyst is in a different phase from the reactants. Example: MnO₂ (solid) catalysing decomposition of H₂O₂ (solution). Involves surface adsorption.
Homogeneous Catalysis
Catalysis where the catalyst is in the same phase as the reactants. Example: acid catalysis in esterification.
Rate of Reaction
The change in concentration (or amount) of a reactant or product per unit time. Affected by temperature, concentration, surface area, and catalysts.
Activation Energy
The minimum energy that colliding particles must have for a reaction to occur. A catalyst lowers the activation energy.
Reversible Reaction
A reaction that can proceed in both the forward and reverse directions. Shown by the ⇌ symbol. Reactants can be formed from products.
Dynamic Equilibrium
When the rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal, so the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant. The reactions haven't stopped - they're occurring at equal rates.
Le Chatelier's Principle
When a system at equilibrium is subjected to a stress (change in concentration, pressure, or temperature), the system shifts to oppose the change and restore equilibrium.
Equilibrium Constant (Kc)
A number that expresses the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, each raised to the power of their coefficients. Only changes with temperature.
Heat of Reaction (ΔH)
The heat change when the amounts shown in a balanced equation react completely. Exothermic = negative ΔH. Endothermic = positive ΔH.
Heat of Combustion
The heat released when one mole of a substance reacts completely with excess oxygen. Always exothermic (negative). Measured using a bomb calorimeter.
Hess's Law
The total enthalpy change for a reaction is the same regardless of the route taken. Allows calculation of ΔH using heats of formation.
Saturated Hydrocarbon
A hydrocarbon that contains only single bonds between carbon atoms. All bonds are sigma bonds. Example: alkanes (methane, ethane, propane).
Unsaturated Hydrocarbon
A hydrocarbon that contains at least one double or triple bond between carbon atoms. Example: alkenes (ethene), alkynes (ethyne).
Fractional Distillation
Separation of a mixture into fractions based on boiling points using a fractionating column. Used to separate crude oil into useful products.
Isomers
Molecules with the same molecular formula but different structural arrangements. Example: butane and methylpropane are both C₄H₁₀.
Homologous Series
A series of compounds with the same general formula, similar chemical properties, and a gradual change in physical properties. Each member differs by CH₂.
Aldehyde
An organic compound with a -CHO functional group. Named with "-al" suffix (e.g., ethanal). Can be oxidised to a carboxylic acid. Reduces Fehling's reagent (blue → red).
Ester
An organic compound formed from the reaction of an alcohol with a carboxylic acid. Named as alkyl alkanoate (e.g., ethyl ethanoate). Often have pleasant smells.
Esterification
The reversible reaction between an alcohol and a carboxylic acid to form an ester and water. Requires an acid catalyst (e.g., H₂SO₄).
Oxidation
Loss of electrons. In organic chemistry: addition of oxygen or removal of hydrogen. Oxidation number increases.
Reduction
Gain of electrons. In organic chemistry: addition of hydrogen or removal of oxygen. Oxidation number decreases.
Electrolysis
The breaking down of a chemical compound using electricity. Requires a molten or dissolved ionic compound and inert electrodes. Oxidation at anode, reduction at cathode.
BOD (Biochemical Oxygen Demand)
The quantity of dissolved oxygen consumed by biological action over 5 days in the dark at 20°C. Measures water pollution - higher BOD = more polluted.
Total Hardness (Water)
The total concentration of calcium and magnesium ions in a water sample. Includes both temporary hardness (removed by boiling) and permanent hardness.
Half-life
The time taken for half the atoms in a radioactive sample to decay. Different for each isotope. Radium-226 has a half-life of 1600 years.
Alpha Particle
A particle consisting of 2 protons and 2 neutrons (a helium nucleus). Emitted during alpha decay. Low penetrating ability - stopped by paper or skin.
Beta Particle
A high-speed electron emitted from the nucleus when a neutron converts to a proton. Medium penetrating ability - stopped by aluminium.
Primary Standard
A substance of known high purity that can be used to standardise (determine the exact concentration of) another solution. Example: anhydrous sodium carbonate, ammonium iron(II) sulfate.
Titration
A technique for determining the concentration of a solution by reacting it with a solution of known concentration. Uses a burette and precise measurements.
End Point
The point in a titration at which the indicator changes colour, showing that the reaction is complete. Should coincide with the equivalence point.

Mark Scheme Decoder

Understanding how Chemistry examiners award marks is crucial for maximising your score.

General Marking Rules

RuleDetail
Key words onlyIn many answers, only specific key words need to appear in the correct context to earn marks.
Arithmetical error-1 mark each time, but only if the candidate shows correct atomic masses and the error is clearly arithmetical.
Missing units-1 mark for omitting units or giving incorrect units in final answers.
Organic structures-1 if H atoms systematically omitted. -1 if bonds systematically omitted.
Names OR formulaeEither the name or formula is accepted for chemical substances, unless otherwise stated. Organic compounds need IUPAC names.
CancellationIf you give more answers than required, or list correct AND incorrect answers, marks may not be awarded.

Q1 - Titration (50 marks)

Primary standard identification (5) - Must name the specific substance. "Ammonium iron(II) sulfate" earns 5 marks.
Procedure descriptions (3+3 each) - Key phrases like "drop by drop / slowly" and "bottom of meniscus on the mark" earn 3 marks each.
Calculations (3+2+2+2+4 = 15) - Each step of the moles calculation earns separate marks. Show EVERY step: n = c×V, then ratio, then scale up, then mass, then %.

Q4 - Short Questions (50 marks)

6 marks per part + 1 bonus for first two attempted - Answer 8 parts. First two attempted get 7 marks each (6+1 bonus). Remaining six get 6 marks each.
Each part typically has 2 elements worth 3 marks each - Example: "by atomic mass (3) similar properties (3)" for Mendeleev's periodic table.
Total: 8 × 6 + 2 × 1 = 50 - Make sure your first two answers are ones you're most confident about to secure the bonus marks.

Q7 - Acids & Bases (50 marks)

Theory definitions (2+2 each) - "Proton donor" (2) and "proton acceptor" (2). These exact phrases are required.
pH calculations (2+2+3) - Formula (2), substitution (2), correct answer (3). Even if answer is wrong, formula earns marks.
Kw expression (6) - Writing Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] earns 6 marks. Pure definition marks.

Q9 - Equilibrium (50 marks)

Kc expression (6) - Writing the correct expression earns 6 marks. Square brackets earn 3 marks, round brackets earn 3.
Kc calculation (2+2+3+3) - Moles of each species (2 each), converting to concentration (3), final Kc value (3).
Le Chatelier's (4+2 each) - State the effect (4) AND justify using Le Chatelier's principle (2). Always explain WHY the shift occurs.

Golden Rules for Maximum Marks

1. Write formulas for calculations. Kc expression, pH = -log[H⁺], n = c×V. The formula alone earns marks even if the calculation goes wrong.
2. Include units in every final answer. mol L⁻¹, kJ mol⁻¹, cm³ min⁻¹. Missing units = -1 mark every time.
3. Use chemical terminology. "Proton donor" not "gives H⁺". "Surface adsorption" not "sticks to the surface". Key words earn marks.
4. Don't over-answer. If asked for ONE advantage, give ONE. Giving two risks cancellation if one is wrong.
5. Show moles calculations step by step. n = mass/Mr, then ratio, then scale. Each step is worth marks.

Q1: Volumetric Analysis - Iron(II) in Iron Tablets 50 marks

A student determined the iron(II) content of iron supplement tablets by dissolving 6 tablets (total 2.496 g) in dilute sulfuric acid, making up to 250 cm³, then titrating 25.0 cm³ portions against 0.010 M KMnO₄.

Reaction: MnO₄⁻ + 5Fe²⁺ + 8H⁺ → Mn²⁺ + 5Fe³⁺ + 4H₂O

(a) 5

Q: Identify a primary standard to standardise the KMnO₄ solution.

Answer: Ammonium iron(II) sulfate / (NH₄)₂SO₄.FeSO₄.6H₂O
Why? A primary standard is a substance of known high purity that you can weigh accurately to make a solution of precise concentration. You then use this to find the exact concentration of the KMnO₄. Common primary standards include anhydrous sodium carbonate and ammonium iron(II) sulfate.

(b)(i) 6

Q: Describe how you would make the solution up to the 250 cm³ mark on the volumetric flask.

Answer: Add deionised water drop by drop / slowly [3] until the bottom of the meniscus is on the 250 cm³ mark [3].
Why drop by drop? If you add too much water, you overshoot the mark and the solution is too dilute. You can't remove water once added. The meniscus is the curved surface of the liquid - you read the bottom of it at eye level.

(b)(ii) 3

Q: Why was more than one tablet used in this experiment?

Answer: Using more tablets gives a smaller percentage error / creates a higher concentration of iron solution, requiring a larger volume of KMnO₄, which reduces the error from a single tablet.

(c)(i) 3

Q: Name a piece of apparatus used to accurately measure 25.0 cm³ of solution.

Answer: Pipette (or burette)

(c)(ii) 6

Q: Describe how you would rinse the pipette before use.

Answer: Rinse first with deionised water [3], then rinse with the iron(II) solution it will contain [3].
Why two rinses? Deionised water removes any contaminants. Then rinsing with the actual solution prevents dilution - if water droplets remain inside and you add iron(II) solution, it would be diluted and your concentration would be wrong.

(d)(i) 3

Q: Why was no indicator needed in this titration?

Answer: Potassium manganate(VII) acts as its own indicator.
Why? KMnO₄ is deep purple. When it reacts with Fe²⁺, it becomes colourless (Mn²⁺). At the end point, there's no more Fe²⁺ to react with, so the next drop of KMnO₄ stays purple - you can see the colour change without any indicator.

(d)(ii) 3

Q: What was observed at the end point of the titration?

Answer: Solution turns (pale) pink and the colour persists.

(d)(iii) 6

Q: What would happen if insufficient acid was used in this titration?

Answer: A brown precipitate [3] would form. This is MnO₂ / Mn⁴⁺ [3] - without enough acid (H⁺), the MnO₄⁻ is only partially reduced to MnO₂ instead of Mn²⁺.

(e) Calculations 15

Given: Average titre = 22.5 cm³ of 0.010 M KMnO₄ | Volume of Fe²⁺ = 25.0 cm³ (from 250 cm³ flask) | 6 tablets, total mass 2.496 g

(i) Moles of MnO₄⁻ in 22.5 cm³

n = c × V / 1000
n = 0.010 × 22.5 / 1000
n = 0.000225 mol = 2.25 × 10⁻⁴ mol [3]

(ii) Moles of Fe²⁺ in 250 cm³ flask

From equation: 1 MnO₄⁻ : 5 Fe²⁺
Moles of Fe²⁺ in 25 cm³ = 0.000225 × 5 = 0.001125 mol [2]
Moles of Fe²⁺ in 250 cm³ = 0.001125 × 10 = 0.01125 mol [2]
Why × 10? The 25 cm³ was taken from a 250 cm³ flask. So the flask contains 250/25 = 10 times as much Fe²⁺ as the sample.

(iii) Average mass of Fe(II) per tablet

Moles per tablet = 0.01125 ÷ 6 = 0.001875 mol [2]
Mass = 0.001875 × 56 = 0.105 g per tablet [2]
Why × 56? The relative atomic mass of iron (Fe) = 56. Mass = moles × Mr.

(iv) Percentage by mass of Fe(II)

Mass of one tablet = 2.496 / 6 = 0.416 g
% = (0.105 / 0.416) × 100 = 25.24% [4]
Alternative method: 0.630 / 2.496 × 100 = 25.24% (using total mass instead of per tablet - same answer).

Q2: Steam Distillation & Extraction of Clove Oil 50 marks

Clove oil is extracted from dried clove buds by steam distillation, then eugenol is isolated by liquid-liquid extraction using cyclohexane.

(a)(i) 3

Q: Identify substance A in the steam distillation setup.

Answer: Water / H₂O
Why? Steam distillation passes steam through the plant material. The steam carries the volatile oil with it. When cooled, you get a mixture of water and oil (the distillate).

(a)(ii) 6

Q: Describe the appearance of the distillate collected.

Answer: Milky [3] appearance - it's an emulsion / oil droplets dispersed in water [3].

(a)(iii) 3

Q: Give one advantage of using steam distillation to extract clove oil.

Answer: Minimises decomposition / maximises yield / removes the oil at a lower boiling point than direct heating would.
Why? Clove oil would decompose if heated to its own boiling point. Steam distillation allows it to evaporate at a temperature below 100°C (steam's boiling point), protecting the delicate oil molecules.

(b)(i) 9

Q: Describe how you would carry out a liquid-liquid extraction to separate the eugenol from the distillate using cyclohexane.

Answer: Pour the distillate into a separating funnel. Add cyclohexane. Shake, vent regularly. Allow layers to separate. The organic layer (cyclohexane + eugenol) forms on top (lower density). Run off the aqueous layer from below. Collect the organic layer in a separate vessel. [max 6+3 marks]
Key point: You earn maximum 6 marks for procedure description even without a diagram. If you include a labelled diagram, you can earn up to 6+3 = 9.

(b)(ii) 6

Q: Give two properties of cyclohexane that make it suitable as a solvent for this extraction.

Answer (any two): Non-polar / low solubility in water [3] | Low boiling point / lower density than water / low reactivity [3]
Why these matter? The solvent must dissolve eugenol (non-polar dissolves non-polar) but NOT mix with water (immiscible). Low boiling point means it's easy to evaporate off later. Lower density means it floats on top for easy separation.

(b)(iii) 3

Q: How could the yield of eugenol be maximised?

Answer: Repeat the extraction / washing of the aqueous layer with fresh cyclohexane.

(b)(iv) 3

Q: How would you isolate the eugenol from the cyclohexane/eugenol mixture?

Answer: Evaporate the solvent (cyclohexane has a low boiling point, so gentle heating removes it, leaving eugenol behind).

(c)(i) 3

Q: Give one use of eugenol.

Answer: Flavouring / antiseptic / fragrance (any one).

(c)(ii)

Q: Draw the structural formula of eugenol.

Answer: Eugenol has a benzene ring with -OH, -OCH₃, and -CH₂CH=CH₂ groups. You must show all atoms and bonds for full marks. -1 for systematic H atom omission, -1 for systematic bond omission.

(c)(iii) 3

Q: Name a spectroscopic technique that could be used to confirm the identity of eugenol.

Answer: Infra-red (IR) / mass spectroscopy / ultra-violet (UV)

(c)(iv) 5

Q: Calculate the expected volume of eugenol from 18.0 g of cloves, given a 7% yield and density of 1.06 g cm⁻³.

Given: 18.0 g of cloves, 7% yield, density of eugenol = 1.06 g cm⁻³
Mass of eugenol = 18.0 × 0.07 = 1.26 g [3]
Volume = mass / density = 1.26 / 1.06 = 1.2 cm³ [2]

Q3: Rate of Reaction - H₂O₂ Decomposition 50 marks

A student monitored the production of O₂ from H₂O₂ using MnO₂ as a catalyst.

Reaction: 2H₂O₂ → O₂ + 2H₂O

Data:

Time (min)0.01.02.03.04.05.06.0
Volume O₂ (cm³)0.060.082.092.098.0100.0100.0

(a)(i) 12

Q: Plot a graph of volume of O₂ (cm³) against time (min) using the data provided.

Marks for: Axes labelled [3] + Points plotted (7×1) [7] + Smooth curve with good fit [2]
Key points: The curve rises steeply at first (fast reaction), then levels off as H₂O₂ is used up. It plateaus at 100 cm³ when all H₂O₂ has decomposed. Draw a smooth curve through the points - don't just join dots with straight lines.

(a)(ii) 6

Q: From your graph, determine the instantaneous rate of reaction at 2.5 minutes.

Method: Draw a tangent to the curve at t = 2.5 min [3]
Rate = slope of tangent ≈ 9.8 cm³ min⁻¹ [3]
What's a tangent? A straight line that just touches the curve at one point. Its slope gives the rate at that exact instant. Rise ÷ Run = change in volume ÷ change in time.

(b)(i) 3

Q: Calculate the average rate of reaction over the first 2.5 minutes.

Average rate = total volume / total time
Read from graph: volume at 2.5 min ≈ 87 cm³
Rate = 87 / 2.5 ≈ 34.8 cm³ min⁻¹ [3]

(b)(ii) 6

Q: If granular MnO₂ was used instead of fine powder, how would the rate of reaction be affected? Explain your answer.

Answer: Rate would decrease [4].
Reason: Granular particles have smaller surface area, so there are fewer collisions between H₂O₂ and the catalyst surface per unit time [2].

(b)(iii) 6

Q: If a more concentrated solution of H₂O₂ was used, how would the rate of reaction be affected? Explain your answer.

Answer: Rate would increase [4].
Reason: More particles per unit volume means more collisions per unit time, so more successful reactions [2].

(c)(i) 3

Q: Calculate the average rate of reaction during the first minute.

Rate = (60.0 - 0.0) / (1.0 - 0.0) = 60 cm³ min⁻¹

(c)(ii) 3

Q: Calculate the average rate of reaction during the second minute.

Rate = (82.0 - 60.0) / (2.0 - 1.0) = 22 cm³ min⁻¹

(c)(iii) 3

Q: Explain why the rate of reaction decreased as the reaction progressed.

Answer: As the reaction proceeds, the concentration of reactants (H₂O₂) decreases, so fewer collisions occur per unit time, and the rate drops.

(d)(i) 4

Q: State how a catalyst increases the rate of a reaction.

Answer: A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy.
Activation energy is the minimum energy needed for a reaction to happen. By lowering it, more particles have enough energy to react, so the reaction goes faster. The catalyst itself is not used up.

(d)(ii) 8

Q: State the type of catalysis taking place and justify your answer.

Answer: Surface adsorption / heterogeneous catalysis [4+2].
Justification: The reactant and catalyst are in different phases [2] - H₂O₂ is in solution (liquid) and MnO₂ is a solid.

Q4: Short Questions 50 marks

Answer eight parts. Each part is worth 6 marks. The first two parts attempted get 1 bonus mark each (7 marks). Total: 8×6 + 2×1 = 50.

(a) 6

Q: State two features of Mendeleev's arrangement of the elements in his periodic table.

Answer: Elements arranged by atomic mass [3] and grouped by similar properties [3].
Key difference from modern table: Mendeleev ordered by atomic mass (not atomic number). He left gaps for undiscovered elements and even predicted their properties.

(b) 6

Q: For the ion ⁵²₂₄Cr³⁺, state the number of (i) protons and (ii) electrons.

(i) Protons = 24 [3] (atomic number = number of protons)
(ii) Electrons = 21 [3] (24 - 3 = 21, because the 3+ charge means 3 electrons lost)

(c) 6

Q: Write the chemical formula for (i) calcium hydroxide and (ii) aluminium sulfate.

(i) Calcium hydroxide: Ca(OH)₂ [3]
(ii) Aluminium sulfate: Al₂(SO₄)₃ [3]
Method: Ca²⁺ needs 2 OH⁻ ions. Al³⁺ needs 3 SO₄²⁻ ions, but since sulfate is 2⁻ and aluminium is 3+, you need 2 Al and 3 SO₄ to balance charges.

(d) 6

Q: Write the electron configuration of vanadium (V, Z=23) using s, p, d notation.

Answer: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s² 3d³ [3+3]

(a) Isotopes of Bromine 20

(i)

Q: Name an instrument used to determine the relative abundance of isotopes.

Answer: Mass spectrometer [2]

(ii)

Q: Compare the nuclei of Br-79 and Br-81 in terms of protons and neutrons.

Both have 35 protons [3]
Br-79 has 44 neutrons, Br-81 has 46 neutrons [3]
How to calculate: Neutrons = mass number - atomic number. For Br-79: 79-35=44. For Br-81: 81-35=46. Same protons (same element), different neutrons (different isotopes).

(iii)

Q: Define relative atomic mass.

Answer: The average mass of an atom of an element [3] compared to one-twelfth the mass of a carbon-12 isotope [3].

(iv)

Q: Calculate the relative atomic mass of bromine given that Br-79 has 50.69% abundance and Br-81 has 49.31% abundance.

(50.69 × 79) + (49.31 × 81) [2]
= 4004.51 + 3994.11 = 7998.62 [2]
7998.62 ÷ 100 = 79.99 [2]

(b) Atomic Orbitals 18

(i)

Q: What is an atomic orbital?

Answer: A region in space around the nucleus where the probability of finding an electron is high [3].

(ii)

Q: What is the shape of the lowest energy orbital?

Answer: Sphere / circle [3] (the 1s orbital is spherical).

(iii)

Q: What is the maximum number of electrons that can occupy a single p-orbital?

Answer: 2 [3] (each orbital holds max 2 electrons with opposite spins).
Note: A p-SUBshell has 3 orbitals and holds 6 electrons. But ONE p-orbital holds only 2.

(iv)

Q: How are electrons arranged when filling orbitals of equal energy?

Answer: Electrons occupy orbitals singly first [3], then pair up [3].
This is Hund's Rule: In a set of equal-energy orbitals (like the three 2p orbitals), each gets one electron before any gets a second. It's like passengers on a bus - everyone takes an empty seat before sitting beside someone.

(v)

Q: Under what circumstances might an electron occupy a higher energy orbital than expected?

Answer: When the atom absorbs a fixed amount of energy [3] (a photon of light or heat), an electron can be promoted to a higher energy level.

(c) Atomic Radius 12

(i)

Q: Define atomic radius.

Answer: Half the distance between the nuclei of two atoms of the same element joined by a single covalent bond [4+4].

(ii)

Q: Explain why the atomic radius of bromine is greater than that of chlorine.

Answer: Bromine has more energy levels than chlorine [2+2]. (Br has 4 energy levels, Cl has 3 - more electron shells means bigger atom.)

(iii)

Q: Explain why the atomic radius of bromine is less than that of selenium.

Answer: Bromine has a greater effective nuclear charge / greater atomic number [2+2]. (Same number of shells, but Br has more protons pulling electrons in more tightly.)

(c) Hess's Law Calculation 14

Reaction: 2NH₃ + H₂O₂ → N₂H₄ + 2H₂O

Heats of formation: NH₃ = -45.9, H₂O₂ = -187.8, N₂H₄ = +50.6, H₂O = -285.8 kJ mol⁻¹

ΔH = Σ ΔHf(products) - Σ ΔHf(reactants)

Products: (+50.6) + 2(-285.8) = 50.6 - 571.6 = -521.0 [3+3]
Reactants: 2(-45.9) + (-187.8) = -91.8 - 187.8 = -279.6 [3+3]
ΔH = -521.0 - (-279.6) = -521.0 + 279.6 = -241.4 kJ [2]
Hess's Law method: ΔH = [sum of ΔHf of products] - [sum of ΔHf of reactants]. Don't forget to multiply by coefficients! The negative answer tells us this reaction is exothermic (releases heat).

(b) pH Calculations 15

(i)

Q: Define pH.

Answer: -log₁₀ [2] of the [H⁺] / moles per litre of H⁺ ions [2].

(ii)

Q: Calculate the concentration of H₃O⁺ ions in a solution with pH = 1.4.

[H⁺] = 10⁻ᵖᴴ = 10⁻¹·⁴ [2]
= 0.0398 mol L⁻¹ [2]

(iii)

Q: Calculate the pH of a 0.015 M solution of a weak acid with Ka = 3.2 × 10⁻⁴.

Given: 0.015 M weak acid, Ka = 3.2 × 10⁻⁴
[H⁺] = √(Ka × M) [2]
= √(3.2 × 10⁻⁴ × 0.015) [2]
= √(4.8 × 10⁻⁶) = 0.00219 mol L⁻¹
pH = -log(0.00219) = 2.66 / 2.7 [3]

(c) Ionic Product of Water 12

(i)

Q: Write an expression for Kw, the ionic product of water.

Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] [6]

(ii)

Q: Calculate the pH of pure water at 60°C, given that Kw = 9.61 × 10⁻¹⁴ mol² L⁻² at that temperature.

Given: Kw at 60°C = 9.61 × 10⁻¹⁴ mol² L⁻²
In pure water: [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = √Kw
[H⁺] = √(9.61 × 10⁻¹⁴) = 3.1 × 10⁻⁷ [3]
pH = -log(3.1 × 10⁻⁷) = 6.5 [3]
Important: Pure water at 60°C has pH 6.5, NOT 7. But it's still neutral! At higher temperatures, Kw increases (more dissociation), so [H⁺] increases and pH drops below 7. But since [H⁺] = [OH⁻], it's still neutral.

(d) Indicators 10

(i)

Q: What colour would the indicator HA display in a strong base? Explain why.

Answer: Blue [3]. Strong base has high pH, so the equilibrium HA ⇌ H₃O⁺ + A⁻ shifts right (Le Chatelier's). More A⁻ (blue) is formed [3].

(ii)

Q: Would the indicator HA (pH range 4.2-5.4) be suitable for this titration? Explain your answer.

Answer: Yes [2]. The indicator changes colour in the pH range 4.2-5.4, and this range coincides with the vertical (steep) portion of the pH curve at the equivalence point [2].
Why it works: At the equivalence point of a strong acid/strong base titration, the pH jumps rapidly (the steep vertical part of the curve). As long as the indicator changes colour within this steep region, it will give an accurate end point.

Q9: Chemical Equilibrium - NOBr Decomposition 50 marks

Reaction: 2NOBr(g) ⇌ 2NO(g) + Br₂(g)

(a) Theory 8

(i)

Q: What is meant by a reversible reaction?

Answer: A reaction where reactants can be formed from products [4]. The reaction can go in both directions.

(ii)

Q: Explain why the concentrations of reactants and products remain constant at equilibrium.

Answer: The rates of the forward and reverse reactions are equal [4]. Reactants are being consumed at the same rate they're being formed.

(b) Kc Calculation 24

Given: 55 g NOBr in 2 L container, 78% decomposed at temperature T.

(i)

Q: Calculate the initial number of moles of NOBr.

Mr of NOBr = 14 + 16 + 80 = 110 [2]
n = 55 / 110 = 0.5 mol [2]

(ii)

Q: Calculate the number of moles of NOBr remaining at equilibrium, given that 78% has decomposed.

Moles decomposed = 0.5 × 0.78 = 0.390 mol [2]
Moles remaining = 0.5 - 0.390 = 0.110 mol [2]

(iii)

Q: Write the expression for Kc for this reaction.

Kc = [NO]²[Br₂] / [NOBr]² [6]
How to write Kc: Products on top, reactants on bottom. Each concentration raised to the power of its coefficient. Square brackets mean "concentration in mol L⁻¹".

(iv)

Q: Calculate the value of Kc at temperature T.

From stoichiometry: 2NOBr → 2NO + Br₂
If 0.390 mol NOBr decompose: 0.390 mol NO formed, 0.195 mol Br₂ formed

Convert to concentrations (÷ 2 L):
[NOBr] = 0.110 / 2 = 0.055 mol L⁻¹ [2]
[NO] = 0.390 / 2 = 0.195 mol L⁻¹ [2]
[Br₂] = 0.195 / 2 = 0.0975 mol L⁻¹ [2]

Kc = (0.195)²(0.0975) / (0.055)² [3]
= 0.038025 × 0.0975 / 0.003025
= 1.2 mol L⁻¹ [3]

(c) Le Chatelier's Principle 18

(i)

Q: State Le Chatelier's Principle.

Answer: When a system at equilibrium [4] is subjected to a stress, it shifts to oppose the applied stress [2].

(ii)

Q: What is the effect of increasing pressure on the amount of Br₂ at equilibrium? Explain your answer.

Answer: Moles of Br₂ decrease [4].
Reason: Increased pressure causes equilibrium to shift to the side with fewer moles of gas [2]. Left side has 2 mol, right side has 3 mol (2NO + Br₂), so shift goes LEFT.

(iii)

Q: Given that Kc increases with temperature, is the forward reaction endothermic or exothermic? Explain.

Given: Kc increases with temperature.
Answer: Endothermic [4].
Reason: If Kc increases with temperature, more products are formed. By Le Chatelier's, the system shifts in the direction that absorbs heat (opposes the temperature increase), which is the forward direction. So the forward reaction is endothermic [2].
